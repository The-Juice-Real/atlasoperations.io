Motion Mongolia should not extract rare earth elements Full Prep Packet

What Is This Debate Really About?

This debate is not about whether rare earth elements are important globally. Everyone agrees they are. The real question is whether Mongolia should authorize commercial REE extraction right now, given the realistic terms on which that extraction would happen and the governance conditions that currently exist.

🔵 PROP MUST PROVE
✦That Mongolia should not authorize commercial rare earth extraction now, because the threshold conditions that justify authorization — safe governance, enforceable liability, and meaningful national capture — have not been met.
✦Prop is not defending a permanent prohibition. The position is a present-policy authorization standard: do not open a high-risk, low-capture sector before the conditions that make authorization justified are in place.
✦The core story: Mongolia would take on the full environmental and governance burden while others capture the high-value margins. That is a bad trade at a bad moment, and a state should not authorize it.
🔴 OPP MUST PROVE
✦That Mongolia should pursue at least some limited, regulated REE extraction. The motion is absolute, so Opp only needs one credible, specific, controlled-entry pathway that is better than blanket refusal.
✦Opp does not need to defend unregulated extraction or promise economic transformation. They only need to prove that a constrained, well-governed extraction model is better than zero participation.
✦The core story: one credible regulated path defeats the motion. Refusal is not neutral — it preserves zero leverage, zero participation, and zero relationships in a market where those things are forming right now.
⚡ The Hidden Fight Inside This Debate

The biggest argument running underneath everything else is whether Proposition is defending a present-policy non-authorization standard (not yet, because conditions aren't met) or something closer to a permanent prohibition. Opposition will try to pin the second reading on Proposition — calling it an "absolutism trap" — because if the motion means "never under any circumstances," it is easy to defeat by describing one plausible future in which conditions have improved.

Proposition must prevent this framing from sticking. The defense is explicit and must be stated early: the motion asks what Mongolia should do as a present policy choice, and the answer is that it should not authorize yet, because the specific threshold conditions — governance capacity, enforceable liability, meaningful capture — do not currently exist. That is a complete answer to a present-policy question. Whichever team frames the motion's scope first and most clearly wins the structural advantage for the entire round.

Best Routes to Win

Know your safest route — the one you can always fall back on — and your highest-reward route for when the round is going well. Each side has four strategic paths mapped here.

✅ Safest Route
Readiness threshold plus low-capture extraction. Anchor the case in two clean, intuitive claims: Mongolia lacks the governance capacity to safely regulate REE's specific waste demands, and without downstream processing, the economic model captures only the weakest margin while Mongolia keeps all the environmental cost.
This route is safe because it requires no speculative or extreme claims. It works even if you grant that rare earths are important, Mongolia has deposits, and global demand is rising. The risk is that Opposition absorbs this framework and argues their crisis cases already satisfy it — which requires Prop to hold the line that their constrained model is aspirational rather than operational.
🎯 Highest-Reward Route
Premature lock-in and bad bargaining sequence. Entering the market before Mongolia has governance capacity and processing leverage does not just produce a bad initial deal — it structurally traps Mongolia in weak contract terms that become the permanent floor, because extraction creates constituencies, commitments, and diplomatic expectations that narrow all future choices.
This argument is powerful because it reframes the debate from a snapshot question (is this a good deal?) to a trajectory question (does this first move lock in a weak position for decades?). The risk is that it can sound abstract without specific examples — Zambia's copper privatization terms are the ideal grounding example here.
📌 Example
Zambia's copper sector privatization in the 1990s and 2000s produced Development Agreements with very low royalty rates, extended tax holidays, and arbitration clauses that made renegotiation legally perilous. When Zambia attempted to raise mining taxes in 2008 and 2015, it faced investment withdrawal threats and arbitration challenges because the initial entry terms had become the structural baseline. This is the premature lock-in trajectory Mongolia risks replicating.
👨‍⚖️ Most Judge-Pleasing Route
Risk stays here, value goes elsewhere. Under the realistic near-term model, Mongolia keeps the waste, the land degradation, the water contamination risk, and the political cost of accidents — while the processing margin, refining margin, and manufacturing margin go to China and others. That asymmetry is not development; it is a bad deal.
Judges respond to moral clarity paired with economic realism. This framing is neither anti-development nor anti-mining — it simply says that a deal where one party carries most of the cost and another captures most of the gain is a bad deal. The sophistication of the framing tends to impress judging panels skeptical of both blanket anti-mining positions and uncritical resource-boom optimism.
🛡 Fallback Route
No pilot without proof. If Opposition narrows their case to a small-scale pilot, Prop holds: a pilot is not made safe by being called a pilot. It is made safe by the specific safeguards — closure bonding, independent monitoring, waste management protocols, enforceable liability — that currently do not exist in place in Mongolia.
Use this when Opp's pilot framing is landing well. Concede that a pilot sounds less alarming than full commercial extraction; then hold that the specific governance conditions required to run even a pilot safely are not currently in place. The question is never the scale — it is always the adequacy of the safeguards.
✅ Safest Route
One credible regulated path defeats the motion. The motion says Mongolia should not extract. One specific, realistic, governed extraction model — limited in scale, with genuine monitoring, strict liability, closure bonds, community consent, and no automatic expansion — defeats that absolute language. Opp does not need perfect extraction; they need one credible counterworld.
This route is safe because it keeps the burden low and the framing honest. Opp is not defending all extraction — they are defending one specific, constrained model that addresses Prop's specific concerns. The more precisely the model is specified, the harder it is for Prop to attack it with general governance concerns.
🎯 Highest-Reward Route
Capacity through controlled practice. The readiness threshold, if taken seriously, creates an impossible standard: Mongolia can only become ready to regulate extraction by doing some regulated extraction. Institutional capacity builds through practice. A constrained pilot is not just extraction — it is a capacity-building exercise aimed at meeting the exact conditions Prop says must be met first.
This route flips Prop's strongest argument. The risk is that it sounds like Mongolia's citizens become experimental subjects for the government's learning process — a vulnerability Prop will exploit aggressively. Opp must frame this as managed learning under constrained exposure, not as letting citizens bear an uncalibrated risk.
📌 Example
Namibia has revised its uranium mining agreements multiple times and progressively increased its revenue share while maintaining investment. Namibia's ability to renegotiate was partly built through accumulated regulatory experience, technical knowledge, and demonstrated compliance track record — none of which can be built without being in the sector. Namibia's renegotiations were hard, but they were possible because the country had a track record to leverage.
👨‍⚖️ Most Judge-Pleasing Route
Comparative mining upgrade. Mongolia is not going to stop being a mining economy in the near term. The realistic choice is not between mining and a better alternative — it is between the current profile (coal-heavy, declining-demand commodities) and a profile that includes more strategically relevant minerals. REE extraction, within a mining economy, is a genuine improvement.
This route is judge-pleasing because it is honest and non-utopian. It does not promise transformation — it claims improvement. Judges who are skeptical of resource-boom optimism still find this argument credible because it stays within realistic limits.
🛡 Fallback Route
Pilot before scale. If Prop is winning against broad commercial extraction, narrow explicitly to a pilot framework with defined scope, defined liability, defined monitoring, defined community consent, and a defined non-expansion default. Prop's general readiness critique loses much of its force against a specific enough model.
Use this when Prop's governance critique is landing against broad extraction but cannot land against limited entry. The key is specificity — a pilot defined concretely is far harder to attack than a pilot described vaguely as "careful" or "phased."

🔵 Prop's Three Arguments

Each argument targets a different dimension of why Mongolia should not authorize REE extraction now. Together they show that the governance conditions are not met, the economic model produces thin capture, and early entry locks in weak terms that become the permanent floor.

01The Readiness Threshold: Mongolia Cannot Yet Safely Govern This Industry's Specific Harms

Authorizing a new industrial sector requires more than identifying that the sector has commercial potential. It requires demonstrating that the state can credibly govern the specific harms that sector creates. Different industries create different harms, and some harms are irreversible if governance fails. Rare earth extraction falls into this category. The ore processing creates toxic wastewater and, in many deposit types, radioactive by-products from naturally occurring thorium and uranium that are chemically co-located with the rare earth minerals. These by-products require specialized containment, continuous monitoring, and long-term management. They seep into groundwater and soil if containment fails, and those harms compound over decades without active intervention.

Mongolia's regulatory infrastructure for mining-related environmental harms is not currently robust enough to govern this specific risk profile. The Oyu Tolgoi copper and gold mine — Mongolia's largest mining project and one of the largest mines in the world, operated with Rio Tinto as a major partner — produced protracted disputes over environmental impact assessments, royalty structures, and benefit-sharing. If Mongolia's system has had difficulty governing a copper mine with one of the world's largest mining companies as a partner, the claim that it is currently prepared to govern REE's considerably more technically demanding waste requirements needs much more support than has been offered.

📌 Real Example
Lynas's rare earth processing facility in Kuantan, Malaysia provides a direct cautionary case. From 2012 onward, the Lynas Advanced Materials Plant operated despite significant unresolved questions about management of Water Leach Purification residue — a low-level radioactive processing by-product. The resulting years of regulatory uncertainty, legal challenges, and community protests illustrate that REE processing creates governance demands that regulatory systems in extractive-economy contexts may not be prepared to handle, and that beginning operations before those questions are resolved makes them harder, not easier, to address once economic interests have been committed.
📌 Counter-Example and Why It Doesn't Win for Opp
Opposition may point to Botswana's diamond industry as evidence that a developing country can build a well-governed extractive sector from a low institutional starting point. Botswana's negotiation of the Debswana joint venture with De Beers, and its use of diamond revenues to build strong state institutions, is a genuine success case. However, diamonds do not produce radioactive waste or the specific governance requirements REE extraction creates. More importantly, Botswana's success resulted from specific institutional conditions — relatively low corruption at independence, strong executive commitment, and decades of careful negotiation — that Mongolia does not currently replicate. Botswana proves that good outcomes are possible under the right conditions, which is precisely what the readiness threshold says: conditions matter, and they must be present before authorization.
💬 Say this: "The readiness threshold is not a general standard of perfect governance applied to all industries equally. It is a specific standard calibrated to this specific industry's specific irreversible harms. A country that cannot govern REE's radioactive by-products safely should not authorize REE extraction — regardless of what it can do with copper or coal."
⛓ So What? — Why This Wins the Motion
→REE extraction creates toxic and radioactive waste by-products requiring specialized, continuous governance to prevent irreversible environmental contamination
→When a state without demonstrated capacity in this area authorizes extraction, monitoring and liability systems that should prevent permanent harm are unreliable or absent
→When those systems are unreliable, the probability of uncontained contamination increases — and contamination in a context without enforceable remediation funding becomes permanent
→The motion asks what Mongolia should do as a present policy choice. Mongolia should not authorize an irreversible-harm industry before it can credibly govern those irreversible harms. That condition is not currently met, so the answer to the motion is: should not.
⚖️ Why This Outweighs Opp's "Readiness Is Not Binary" Response
Opposition's best counter is that readiness is not binary — a tightly controlled, limited-entry model with international monitoring and closure bonding addresses the governance problem rather than ignoring it. This counter has real force. The asymmetry that favors Proposition is this: Opp's argument depends on the credibility of the controls, and credibility requires institutional foundations that must be specified, not assumed. "Tightly controlled with genuine oversight" is a description of what Opp wants — not an account of what Mongolia currently has. In a context where harms are irreversible, the cost of false caution (a little lost time and opportunity) is much lower than the cost of false confidence (communities harmed permanently). That asymmetry is decisive when harms cannot be undone.
🧑‍⚖️ Likely Judge Objection — Answer This Before They Think It
"Isn't this just saying Mongolia needs better governance before it does anything? By that standard, no developing country would ever industrialize."
The readiness threshold is not a general governance standard applied to all industries equally. It is a specific standard applied because REE extraction creates harms that are hard to reverse and compound over time without continuous monitoring. Most industries do not have this property. A textile factory in a country with weak regulatory capacity creates risks — but those risks are visible, confined, and do not persist indefinitely without active management. REE tailings seep into groundwater for decades. The threshold is calibrated to this specific risk profile, not to a general standard of perfect governance before any industry is allowed.
🔴 Opp will say
Readiness is not binary. Controlled entry under a constrained model — international monitoring, closure bonding, liability structures — addresses the readiness concern rather than ignoring it. You are describing an impossible standard.
🔵 Your defense
The credibility of the controls depends on institutional foundations that must be specified, not assumed. Prop's standard is: show us the system before you open the sector. In a context where harms are irreversible, that burden is correctly placed on the institution seeking to open the sector, not on those asking whether it is safe to do so.
02Low-Capture Extraction: Mongolia Would Keep the Burden While Others Keep the Margin

Even setting governance aside, a separate and decisive question is whether the economic model for Mongolian REE extraction actually delivers meaningful benefit to Mongolia. The rare earth supply chain has a distinctive structure: extraction is the cheapest, dirtiest, and least profitable stage. Processing, separation, refining, and manufacturing of finished components — the permanent magnets, battery materials, and phosphors that actually command high prices — capture the large margins. China spent decades building dominance in REE processing precisely because it understood that the real value is not in the ground. It is in what you do after you get the material out of the ground.

Mongolia currently has no significant rare earth processing infrastructure. It also lacks the industrial base, technical workforce, energy infrastructure, and capital to build that processing capacity in the near term. This is a factual description of current conditions, not a permanent verdict on Mongolia's potential. Given that description, the realistic near-term model is that Mongolia digs up ore and ships it elsewhere — very likely China, given geography and the concentration of processing capacity — and receives the upstream payment. The upstream payment is the weakest part of the value chain. Mongolia keeps the waste. Mongolia keeps the land degradation. The processing margin, refining margin, and manufacturing margin go elsewhere.

📌 Real Example
The Democratic Republic of Congo contains an estimated 70 percent of the world's cobalt reserves — a mineral in very high demand for electric vehicle batteries. Despite this extraordinary endowment, the DRC captures only the raw ore price. Refiners in China and finished goods manufacturers in South Korea and Japan capture the downstream margins. The DRC's cobalt industry has been associated with significant environmental and labor harms, and the country's population has not seen development transformation consistent with its mineral wealth. The lesson is not that resource extraction is always bad — it is that raw material extraction without downstream leverage produces thin capture, and thin capture in a high-harm industry is a bad trade.
📌 Counter-Example and Why It Doesn't Win for Opp
Opposition may argue that Chile's copper industry shows a country can export primarily raw or semi-processed commodities and still use revenues to build strong institutions. Chile has done this with copper through Codelco. However, Chile has had decades to build Codelco into a sophisticated state institution with genuine technical capacity and strong royalty arrangements — and even Chile still faces criticism that over-reliance on raw copper export has constrained its development. If Chile, with decades of experience and a high-volume commodity with transparent pricing, still wrestles with the low-capture problem, Mongolia's prospects for solving it quickly with a new, technically demanding mineral in a less favorable governance context are considerably weaker.
💬 Say this: "A strategic asset can still be sold badly. The world wanting rare earths is not the same as Mongolia receiving good terms for producing them. The question is not whether the mineral has value — it is who captures that value and who bears the cost."
⛓ So What? — Why This Wins the Motion
→Without downstream processing capacity, Mongolia's REE revenue reflects only the upstream extraction margin — the weakest margin in the supply chain
→The benefit side of the authorization calculation must be done against the realistic revenue figure, not the full value of the rare earth market — and when done honestly, the benefit shrinks considerably
→When thin upstream revenues are measured against governance burden, waste liability, land degradation, and political cost of any accidents, the net figure may not be positive
→The motion asks whether Mongolia should extract. The answer requires a realistic calculation. If extraction creates serious governance demands and produces thin economic capture, the case for authorization is not clearly positive — and authorizing an industry where the interests case is not clearly positive fails the basic standard for state authorization of a high-risk sector.
⚖️ Why This Outweighs Opp's "Partial Capture Beats Zero" Response
Opposition's strongest counter is that even a thin upstream margin is more than zero — more than Mongolia gets from leaving the deposits in the ground. This is the most honest and difficult version of the counter. The response is twofold. First, leaving deposits in the ground is not the same as capturing zero — it preserves optionality. As Mongolia develops processing capacity and the diplomatic context shifts, extracting later from a stronger position may produce substantially higher returns than extracting now from the weakest position. Second, the calculation is not revenues versus zero — it is revenues versus costs. If thin upstream revenues are offset against governance burden, waste management liability, and opportunity cost of administrative attention, the net figure may not be positive. Partial capture is only better than zero if the costs of extraction are low enough for the margin to survive them. Opp has not shown that.
🧑‍⚖️ Likely Judge Objection — Answer This Before They Think It
"Isn't this just saying Mongolia isn't developed enough to benefit from its own resources? Isn't that a kind of paternalism that conveniently keeps developing countries out of markets?"
This is not a paternalistic argument — it is a strategic one. The same logic applies to any negotiator: entering a deal before you have leverage produces worse outcomes than entering after you have leverage. The argument is not "Mongolia cannot benefit." It is "Mongolia will benefit more if it enters when it has more to offer than raw ore." That is strategic advice, not a denial of capacity or a permanent exclusion from the market.
🔴 Opp will say
Partial capture still beats zero. Entry into the market builds relationships and leverage that eventually improve future bargaining. You can't get to downstream capacity without first building upstream presence.
🔵 Your defense
The comparison is not revenues versus zero — it is revenues versus costs. And optionality is not zero either: entering later from a stronger position produces better returns than entering now from the weakest one. Opp needs to show why the window closes before conditions improve. They have not done that.
03Premature Lock-In: Bad Entry Terms Become the Permanent Floor

The third reason Mongolia should not authorize REE extraction now is about what happens to Mongolia's future choices once extraction begins. Authorizing commercial extraction is not a reversible decision in any practical sense. Once it happens, a chain of consequences follows that narrows future options significantly. Contracts are signed with stabilization clauses and dispute resolution mechanisms that are difficult to renegotiate. Infrastructure is built — roads, processing facilities, tailings storage — creating financial and operational commitments. Diplomatic expectations are established with buyer countries who now have interests in Mongolia's extraction continuing. Political constituencies form domestically around the jobs, royalties, and contracts the industry creates, making future regulation more politically costly. All of these things together mean that the terms of early entry tend to become the permanent baseline, not a temporary starting point that can be improved easily.

The risk is not only getting a bad initial deal. It is getting a bad initial deal that becomes structurally difficult to leave or renegotiate because the economic, political, and diplomatic architecture of the sector was built around those terms. Mongolia's ability to regulate more aggressively, renegotiate more favorably, or exit the sector if circumstances deteriorate is substantially constrained by whatever terms govern the initial authorization.

📌 Real Example
Zambia's copper belt illustrates how early entry terms shape a country's economic trajectory for decades. The Development Agreements signed during the 1990s–2000s privatization included very low royalty rates, extended tax holidays, and arbitration clauses making renegotiation legally and financially perilous. When Zambia attempted to increase mining taxes in 2008 and again in 2015, it faced investment withdrawal threats and international arbitration challenges — because the initial authorization terms had become the structural baseline from which all future policy had to negotiate. The lock-in was not a deliberate trap; it was the predictable consequence of entering when leverage was weakest and commitments were most extensive.
📌 Counter-Example and Why It Doesn't Win for Opp
Opposition may argue that Namibia's uranium sector shows contracts can be renegotiated and early entry does not necessarily mean permanent disadvantage. Namibia has revised uranium mining agreements and progressively increased revenue shares. However, Namibia's renegotiations were difficult, took years, and required significant political capital. More importantly, Namibia has relatively strong institutions by regional standards and a smaller, more manageable mining sector. The fact that lock-in can occasionally be partially reversed through sustained institutional effort does not undermine the argument — it confirms that reversing it is costly and slow. That is exactly the case for not entering on weak terms in the first place.
💬 Say this: "The question is not just whether this is a good deal in year one. It is whether this first move puts Mongolia in a stronger or weaker position for every year after. If it locks in the weakest terms at the moment of lowest leverage, that is not a starting point — it is a ceiling."
⛓ So What? — Why This Wins the Motion
→When a state authorizes extraction before it has governance capacity and bargaining leverage, it enters contracts under weak terms that create economic and political commitments making renegotiation or exit costly
→Once those commitments exist, the terms of early entry tend to become the structural baseline for the sector — Mongolia's future ability to capture more value, regulate more effectively, or exit is substantially constrained
→The motion is not asking about year one of extraction. It is asking whether Mongolia should open this sector — a decision that shapes the entire trajectory
→If the trajectory is locked in at the weakest starting point, the authorization decision must be judged by reference to the full trajectory, not just the opening moment — and on that judgment, Mongolia should not authorize now.
⚖️ Why This Outweighs Opp's "Lock-In Is Not Inevitable" Response
Opposition's best counter is that a properly designed entry model — with sunset clauses, renegotiation triggers, no automatic expansion — can prevent path dependency. This counter has theoretical merit. The problem is that it depends on the same governance capacity that the readiness threshold argument says Mongolia currently lacks. You cannot simultaneously argue that Mongolia's regulatory institutions are strong enough to design and enforce genuinely lock-in-resistant contracts and that the readiness threshold concern about Mongolia's governance capacity is overblown. The two arguments pull against each other, and one of them has to give.
🧑‍⚖️ Likely Judge Objection — Answer This Before They Think It
"Doesn't this argument apply to every first-time sector entry? By this logic, Mongolia should never enter any new industry because there's always a risk of bad early contract terms."
The lock-in argument has special force in REE extraction for two reasons that do not apply to most sectors. First, the harms are irreversible — if early terms turn out to be bad, Mongolia cannot simply close the mine and restore the status quo. The environmental damage persists. Second, supply chain concentration in REE processing means Mongolia is entering a buyer's market where the processing capacity is dominated by a small number of actors. That structural feature gives Mongolia particularly weak leverage at entry, making the lock-in risk particularly serious. These two factors — irreversible harm plus structural buyer dominance — are not present in most first-time sector entries.
🔴 Opp will say
Lock-in is not inevitable under a well-designed limited-entry model. Sunset clauses, renegotiation triggers, and no automatic expansion provisions prevent path dependency. Your argument proves bad contract design is bad — not that controlled entry locks Mongolia in.
🔵 Your defense
Well-designed lock-in-resistant contracts require the same institutional capacity the readiness argument says Mongolia currently lacks. Those two arguments pull against each other. Opp cannot use both simultaneously — if Mongolia can design and enforce sophisticated contracts, the readiness concern weakens; if it cannot, the lock-in risk is real.

🔴 Opp's Three Arguments

Opp's formula is consistent across all three arguments: acknowledge Prop's specific governance concerns as real — because they are — then show why a specific, constrained, well-governed extraction model addresses those concerns better than blanket refusal. The key is specificity. Vague assurances of "regulation" lose. Named mechanisms win.

01One Credible Regulated Path Defeats the Absolute Motion

The starting point for everything Opposition argues is the structure of the motion itself. The motion says Mongolia should not extract rare earth elements — full stop, no qualifiers. This is a universal negative. A universal claim of this form is defeated if any meaningful, real, credible counterexample exists. Opposition does not need to prove that REE extraction is the best thing Mongolia could do. They need only prove that there is at least one extraction pathway — specific, realistic, governed by genuine constraints — under which Mongolia should proceed. If that pathway is credible, the motion fails on its own terms.

The pathway Opposition defends has five features. It is limited in scale — covering specific defined deposits under defined geographic and production limits, not blanket sector-wide development. It is subject to genuine independent environmental monitoring — not self-reported by the extracting company, but measured and reported by a third party with real reporting obligations. It includes strict liability provisions — the company and its principals are legally and financially responsible for remediation costs. It requires a closure bond — an upfront financial deposit held by a neutral party, sufficient to fund remediation if the company exits. And it contains no automatic expansion clause — scale-up requires a new authorization decision, not a default. This is not an optimistic model. It is a conservative model built around the specific risks Proposition has identified.

📌 Real Example
Namibia's management of its uranium sector — including the Rössing mine, in which the Namibian government holds a direct equity stake through related government structures — offers a partial example of how a country in a developing-economy context can maintain meaningful oversight of a technically demanding extractive industry. Namibia is not a perfect model, and its institutions differ from Mongolia's. But the example establishes that the choice is not binary between pristine Western-standard regulation and total governance failure. A functional constrained-entry model can operate in the space between those poles.
📌 Counter-Example and Why It Doesn't Win for Prop
Proposition will point to the Lynas facility in Malaysia as evidence that REE processing in a developing-economy context produces governance failures even under close scrutiny. This is a legitimate concern. However, the Lynas case does not defeat the one-credible-path argument for a specific reason: the Lynas facility was a processing plant, not a mine, and its governance challenges arose from management of radioactive processing residues in a densely populated coastal area with complex political dynamics around nuclear materials. A limited extraction operation in Mongolia's sparsely populated mineral belt, with proper tailings design, closure bonding, and independent monitoring, presents a different risk profile. The Lynas case proves governance requirements are real and must be taken seriously — which Opp's model does. It does not prove those requirements cannot be met.
💬 Say this: "Prop attacks bad extraction. We defend controlled extraction. Those are different things, and Prop must show why our specific model fails — not why some other extraction model is harmful."
⛓ So What? — Why This Wins the Motion
→A constrained, liability-anchored, independently monitored extraction model addresses Prop's specific governance and capture concerns while generating revenue, geopolitical relevance, and institutional learning that complete refusal cannot provide
→If even one such model is credible and deliverable, the absolute negative stated in the motion is wrong
→Prop's burden is not just to prove bad extraction is bad — they already won that argument before they stood up. Their burden is to prove that no version of extraction, including the tightly constrained version Opp has described, is something Mongolia should do
→"Should not" is an absolute claim, and one credible counterexample defeats an absolute claim. Opp has provided the counterexample. The motion fails.
⚖️ Why This Outweighs Prop's "Your Model Depends on Capacity Mongolia Doesn't Have" Response
Prop's best counter is that the constrained model relies on institutional conditions — monitoring capacity, liability enforcement, political will to hold companies accountable — that Mongolia currently lacks. The asymmetry that favors Opposition is this: the constrained model is specifically designed to reduce the dependence on domestic institutional capacity by requiring international monitoring, mandatory closure bonding, and legal structures with international enforcement mechanisms. The claim is not "trust Mongolia's current institutions." It is "design the entry model so that it does not depend entirely on trusting Mongolia's current institutions." That is a meaningful and specific response to the readiness concern — not an evasion of it.
🧑‍⚖️ Likely Judge Objection — Answer This Before They Think It
"Who actually enforces the safeguards Opp is describing? If Mongolia's institutions are weak, where does enforcement come from?"
Enforcement in international mining deals can be structured to run through multiple overlapping channels simultaneously: binding contracts with international arbitration clauses, independent third-party monitoring bodies with genuine reporting obligations, closure bonds held by neutral financial institutions, and diplomatic conditionality from partner governments. No single channel is perfect. Together, they create overlapping accountability structures that reduce but do not eliminate risk. The honest version of this argument is not that the model is perfectly safe — it is that the model distributes and limits risk in a way that makes controlled entry better than no entry.
🔵 Prop will say
Your constrained model sounds careful on paper but depends on institutional capacity — monitoring, liability enforcement, political will — that Mongolia currently lacks. You are describing what you want, not what exists.
🔴 Your defense
The model is specifically designed to not depend entirely on domestic institutions — by requiring international monitoring, mandatory closure bonding, and international arbitration. The claim is not "trust Mongolia's current institutions." It is "design the model so it doesn't need to."
02Strategic Relevance Requires Entry — Deposits Without Participation Generate No Leverage

Mongolia's REE deposits are currently geologically relevant — they exist, they are significant, and researchers and investors know about them. But geological relevance is not the same as geopolitical relevance. A country that sits on a resource but does not participate in its market is, from the perspective of buyers, partners, and investors, simply an unmoved option. Buyers develop other supply chains. Investment goes to producing countries. The diplomatic relationships that generate favorable trade conditions, infrastructure partnerships, and political leverage form around actual suppliers — not around potential ones.

REE demand has continued to grow, driven primarily by the global transition toward electric vehicles and renewable energy infrastructure. REE refining remains highly concentrated in China. Countries including the United States, Japan, South Korea, and EU members have significant stated policy interests in diversifying REE supply away from China. Mongolia, as a potential non-Chinese supplier with substantial deposits, has something those partners want. But that strategic relevance only translates into real leverage if Mongolia is an actual or credible near-term supplier. A country that says "we have the deposits but we've decided not to extract" does not generate the same partnership interest, diplomatic capital, or infrastructure investment as a country that says "we are developing a responsible extraction program and we are looking for partners."

📌 Real Example
Japan's response to China's 2010 rare earth export restrictions illustrates the demand for alternative supply with precision. In September 2010, following a diplomatic incident involving a Chinese fishing vessel near disputed islands, China informally restricted rare earth exports to Japan. Japanese manufacturers experienced supply disruptions and price spikes. Japan responded by investing heavily in rare earth recycling, material substitution research, and partnerships with alternative supplier countries — including Australia, Canada, and India. Japan's willingness to invest in and give preferential terms to alternative REE suppliers is direct evidence that participation in the market generates strategic relationships that non-participation does not. Mongolia, bordering China with significant deposits, is theoretically well-positioned to attract Japanese partnership interest — but only as a plausible supplier.
📌 Counter-Example and Why It Doesn't Win for Prop
Prop may argue that Australia's experience with China's 2020 informal trade restrictions on coal, barley, and other commodities shows that being a resource supplier creates political vulnerability, not leverage. This is a valid general principle. However, it does not defeat the strategic relevance argument here for a specific reason: the concern about vulnerability to a dominant buyer is exactly why diversified partner relationships matter. Mongolia's goal should not be to become China's exclusive REE supplier — that would replicate the dependency problem. The goal is to become a supplier with multiple buyer relationships, precisely to avoid that vulnerability. Entry is a precondition for building those diversified relationships. Staying out leaves no relationships and no leverage at all.
💬 Say this: "A map attracts curiosity. A supplier attracts leverage. Mongolia's deposits generate awareness. Only participation generates the partnerships, infrastructure, and diplomatic capital that make awareness worth anything."
⛓ So What? — Why This Wins the Motion
→REE demand is growing and existing supply concentration creates a structural buyer preference for alternative suppliers — actual or near-term suppliers attract diplomatic partnerships and infrastructure investment that non-suppliers do not
→Mongolia, by refusing to extract, forgoes the specific diplomatic and strategic relationships its deposits could generate — relationships that might include infrastructure investment, processing technology partnerships, and preferential trade terms
→Those relationships are forming now, as buyer countries make supply chain partner decisions in a specific window of time
→The motion asks whether Mongolia should not extract. If participation generates real strategic relationships and non-participation generates none, and if those relationships are forming now, the cost of non-participation is not zero — and the motion requires a judgment about whether the cost of participation outweighs that cost. It does not.
⚖️ Why This Outweighs Prop's "Global Importance Doesn't Prove Mongolian Benefit" Response
Prop's best counter is that global demand for rare earths does not automatically mean Mongolia will receive good terms for supplying them. This is correct as a logical observation. The asymmetry that favors Opposition is this: the strategic relevance argument is not claiming that participation automatically produces a good deal. It is claiming that participation is a precondition for any deal at all. The difference between a bad deal and no deal is that a bad deal can be improved through renegotiation, political pressure, and accumulated leverage. No deal produces zero and cannot be improved. Even imperfect participation creates the platform on which better terms can eventually be built. Total non-participation does not create that platform.
🧑‍⚖️ Likely Judge Objection — Answer This Before They Think It
"Hasn't Mongolia already demonstrated some strategic importance just by having the deposits? Don't diplomatic conversations already happen? Does participation really add that much leverage?"
Geological awareness generates some diplomatic interest — investors and governments do track Mongolia's REE potential. But that interest is qualitatively different from the interest generated by actual supply. An unmined deposit is a future option that might be exercised. An active, regulated extraction program is a current economic partner. The latter generates investment, infrastructure, technology transfer, and diplomatic commitment in a way the former does not. The question is not whether Mongolia has zero strategic relevance without extraction. It is whether that relevance is substantially enhanced by participation. The evidence — from Japan's investment patterns, from the EU's Critical Raw Materials Act priorities, from US Defense Production Act investments in REE supply — strongly suggests it is.
🔵 Prop will say
Global importance does not prove Mongolian net benefit. The world wanting rare earths and Mongolia receiving good terms for supplying them are two entirely different claims. You have proven the first and assumed the second.
🔴 Your defense
Participation is a precondition for any deal at all. The comparison is not good deal versus better deal. It is imperfect deal — which can be improved over time — versus no deal, which produces zero and cannot be improved. Entry creates the platform. Non-entry does not.
03Comparative Mining Upgrade: Within Mongolia's Realistic Baseline, REEs Are Better Than the Alternative

The most honest of Opposition's arguments starts by accepting a baseline fact: Mongolia is a mining economy and will remain one for the foreseeable future. Mining dominates Mongolia's exports, with coal and copper as the dominant current commodities. A debate motion asking whether Mongolia should extract rare earth elements is not a debate about whether Mongolia should be a mining country. That question is not on the table in the near term. The real choice available to Mongolia is not between mining and something better. It is between the current mining profile and a future mining profile that might include more strategically valuable minerals.

Within that realistic framework, REE extraction may represent a genuine improvement. Rare earths are more strategically relevant than coal in current global demand terms — coal is under declining demand pressure from the energy transition while rare earths are under rising demand pressure from the same transition. The diplomatic relationships available to an REE supplier are different from, and in some respects better than, those available to a coal exporter. Refusing rare earths does not create a post-extractive economy. It simply removes an improvement opportunity from the available option set while the current commodity mix continues.

📌 Real Example
Chile's deliberate effort to develop a lithium industry alongside its copper industry offers an instructive parallel. Chile has been a copper exporter for a long time and copper has driven its economic profile with all the associated dependencies. When lithium became strategically important for electric vehicle batteries, Chile moved to capture that market — partly through state involvement via CODELCO and the National Lithium Company announced in 2023. Chile explicitly framed this as strategic diversification within mining, not as an exit from mining. The goal was to add a more forward-looking, higher-value mineral to a portfolio that already depended on extractive revenues. The same logic applies to Mongolia: if mining is the baseline, adding strategically valuable minerals is better than remaining dependent on the least strategically relevant ones.
📌 Counter-Example and Why It Doesn't Win for Prop
Prop may argue that changing the mineral does not change the model — that adding rare earths to coal and copper simply means more extraction with a different label, and the fundamental low-capture, governance, and dependency problems remain unaddressed. This is a coherent critique. However, it does not defeat the comparative mining upgrade argument because that argument does not claim to solve the structural problem. It claims to make the existing structure better. A comparative argument does not need to prove transformation — it only needs to prove improvement. Even if REE extraction does not fix Mongolia's extractive dependency, if it produces more strategic relevance, better diplomatic partnerships, and marginally higher long-term leverage than the current commodity mix, it is a better option within the available set.
💬 Say this: "Refusing rare earths does not create a post-extractive economy. It preserves a coal-heavy, declining-demand one. Within the realistic options available to Mongolia now, an improvement is still an improvement."
⛓ So What? — Why This Wins the Motion
→Mongolia's baseline is a commodity-dependent mining economy dominated by coal and copper — this is not going to change in the near term regardless of what happens with REE extraction
→REE extraction within that baseline adds strategic relevance, new partnership opportunities, and a more forward-facing commodity to the existing mix
→The alternative — blanket non-participation — does not change the baseline. It removes an improvement opportunity from the available options
→The motion requires a comparison with the realistic alternative. When the realistic alternative is continued coal and copper dependence with no new strategic direction, refusing REE extraction is not a step toward something better — it is a step toward more of the same. That comparison does not support the motion.
⚖️ Why This Outweighs Prop's "More Mining Is Still More Mining" Response
Prop's best counter is that this argument proves only that the status quo is bad, not that REE extraction is good — that the solution to coal and copper dependence is to develop other industries, not to add more mining. This counter is intellectually attractive but practically empty in a short-form debate. The question is not what Mongolia's ideal long-run structural transformation looks like — no one disputes that reducing extractive dependency is better over a long enough horizon. The question is what Mongolia should do now, under current conditions. The near-term alternatives to mining-led growth are not adequately specified by Proposition. The asymmetry: Opposition is offering a real improvement within the available option set, and Proposition is responding with an ideal that is not available in the near term.
🧑‍⚖️ Likely Judge Objection — Answer This Before They Think It
"This argument seems to accept everything that's wrong with Mongolia's economic model and then say 'well, rare earths are slightly less bad.' Why should the court accept that an upgrade within a bad system is sufficient justification for opening a new mine?"
The relevant standard in this debate is not whether REE extraction is ideal — it is whether it is better than the realistic alternative. The realistic alternative is the current model. In that comparison, a genuine improvement is still an improvement. The motion does not ask whether Mongolia should prioritize REE extraction above all other development strategies. It asks whether Mongolia should not extract. An argument that controlled extraction is a genuine improvement over the realistic non-extraction baseline, within the constraints Mongolia actually faces, is sufficient to defeat that motion.
🔵 Prop will say
This argument just adds more extraction and calls it progress. Changing the mineral doesn't change the model. The solution to extractive dependence is not more extraction — it's investing in other sectors.
🔴 Your defense
A comparative argument doesn't need to prove transformation — it needs to prove improvement. And the comparison is not between REE extraction and an ideal diversified economy. It's between REE extraction and continued coal-heavy dependence. In that comparison, improvement is still improvement.

⚡ The 5 Big Clashes

These are the five points where the two sides most directly contradict each other. Winning the debate means winning at least two or three of these convincingly. Know before the round which clashes your side has the easier burden on, and prepare for the ones where the burden is harder.

Prop Has Slight EdgeClash 1 — Readiness vs. Paralysis
Prop Must Win This By Showing
That the governance gap is real and specific — not a vague claim about general institutional weakness, but an account of the particular monitoring, liability, and enforcement mechanisms that REE extraction requires and that Mongolia currently lacks. And that this specific gap cannot be bridged by contract design alone without institutional foundations Mongolia currently lacks.
Opp Must Win This By Showing
That the constrained entry model is genuinely different from unconstrained extraction in its risk profile — that the specific governance requirements Prop has identified can be met through international monitoring, closure bonding, and contractual liability structures that do not depend entirely on Mongolia's current domestic institutions. This must be concrete, not aspirational.
⚖️ How a Judge Decides This Clash
Has Prop named a specific governance gap and explained why it creates irreversible harm? Has Opp specified how their model addresses that specific gap? Prop wins if they name a specific gap and Opp responds only with general assurance of oversight. Opp wins if they specify concrete mechanisms and Prop cannot show why those mechanisms are insufficient. The fatal mistake for Prop: staying at general institutional weakness without drilling to specific irreversible harms. The fatal mistake for Opp: staying at general assurance without specifying mechanism. Named mechanisms — closure bond types, monitoring protocols, liability structures — are what win this clash for Opp.
Opp Has Easier BurdenClash 2 — Strategic Asset vs. Bad Bargain
Prop Must Win This By Showing
That strategic value and good terms are separable — Mongolia can possess the first without guaranteeing the second. The specific structural features of the REE market — processing concentration, buyer leverage, absence of transparent spot pricing — make early-entry terms specifically likely to be weak. And that Mongolia's strategic relevance will still be available later, when it enters from a stronger position.
Opp Must Win This By Showing
That strategic relevance is time-sensitive — that supply chain relationships, infrastructure investments, and partner commitments being formed now are not indefinitely open. Partner countries are making supplier decisions in a specific current window. A Mongolia that is absent from the market during this period risks being excluded from the networks that are forming.
⚖️ How a Judge Decides This Clash
The judge looks for evidence of time-sensitivity. Opp wins if they can point to concrete policy decisions — US Defense Production Act REE investments, Japan's rare earth partnership programs, EU Critical Raw Materials Act — as windows that close. Prop wins if they can show Mongolia can join those networks later from a stronger position. The fatal mistake for Prop: implying strategic relevance can be preserved indefinitely without participation. The fatal mistake for Opp: claiming the window is closing without showing why Mongolia specifically cannot enter later once conditions improve.
Even FightClash 3 — Low Capture vs. Partial Gain
Prop Must Win This By Showing
That the relevant comparison is not revenues versus zero, but revenues versus costs. Must specify the costs — environmental management, regulatory administration, political liability, opportunity cost of administrative attention — and argue those costs plausibly match or exceed the thin upstream margin. Must also challenge the claim that early entry builds leverage rather than locking in starting terms.
Opp Must Win This By Showing
That even the thin upstream margin is positive after realistic cost accounting. The stronger version: partial capture is not static — entry creates the platform for progressive bargaining improvement in ways non-entry cannot. Must name specific mechanisms by which participation generates future leverage: technical knowledge, regulatory precedent, diplomatic relationships, compliance track record.
⚖️ How a Judge Decides This Clash
The judge looks for which side gave a more honest and specific account of costs and benefits. A vague "some value is better than none" loses to a specific cost account. A vague "readiness gap" loses to a specific account of how partial capture compounds over time. The fatal mistake for Prop: failing to specify the costs claimed to offset revenues. The fatal mistake for Opp: relying on "partial capture is still positive" without addressing the cost side of the ledger at all.
Even FightClash 4 — More Mining vs. Better Mining
Prop Must Win This By Showing
That REE extraction within Mongolia's economy is not genuinely comparative — that the specific additional harms (governance demands, waste profile, radioactive by-product risk) are meaningfully different from and worse than the existing commodity mix, and that the upgrade argument fails because it brings genuinely new and worse risks rather than substituting one similar commodity for another.
Opp Must Win This By Showing
The comparison honestly. Not that REEs are the same as copper in risk profile — they are not — but that the strategic improvement in diplomatic relationships, market positioning, and long-run leverage is large enough to justify the additional complexity, and that the additional risks are manageable through the constrained entry model. Must specify what the strategic improvement actually consists of.
⚖️ How a Judge Decides This Clash
The judge looks for which side engages more honestly with the actual comparison. Prop wins if they can show REEs add meaningfully worse governance demands without proportionately better strategic gains. Opp wins if they can show the strategic improvements are specific and the additional risks are manageable. The fatal mistake for Prop: treating all mining as identical in risk profile when the governance demands clearly vary. The fatal mistake for Opp: treating all mining as identical in strategic value when the case for REEs specifically needs to be made.
Opp Has Easier Burden By DefaultClash 5 — The Absolute Motion
Prop Must Win This By Showing
That defending a present non-authorization standard is a complete answer to an absolute motion. The motion asks what Mongolia should do as a present policy choice, and the answer is "should not authorize yet, because the threshold conditions do not currently exist." This is not "never" — it is a direct answer to the present-policy question the motion asks.
Opp Must Win This By Showing
That "should not" in the motion does mean a present and ongoing prohibition, and that Prop's "not now" defense is actually an implicit concession that conditions could in principle be met — which means the debate is about conditions, not a categorical prohibition, and in a conditions debate Opp wins by showing one credible condition set under which extraction is justified.
⚖️ How a Judge Decides This Clash
This clash is decided by the judge's interpretation of the motion's scope. A judge reading "should not" as a present-policy question will be receptive to Prop's threshold framing. A judge reading it as carrying an absolute implication will be receptive to Opp's one-credible-path argument. Because judges differ, both sides need to engage with both interpretations. The fatal mistake for Prop: relying entirely on the threshold framing without showing the threshold is not currently met. The fatal mistake for Opp: relying entirely on the one-credible-path framing without actually specifying the path in enough detail to be evaluated concretely.

💬 Best Questions to Ask

A good POI exposes a gap the other side would rather not address, or forces them to say something out loud that damages their own case. Have your top three ready from memory and deliver them calmly — the best POIs feel like honest questions, not attacks.

🔵
Prop's Best POIs
▼
⚔️ Offensive — ask these to attack their case
  • 01On value capture: "If Mongolia lacks downstream processing, where exactly is the high-value capture in your extraction model? What specific part of the supply chain generates the margin you are claiming Mongolia will receive?"
  • 02On safeguard credibility: "What makes your model's safeguards credible rather than aspirational? Can you name the specific mechanism that closes the tailings ponds, monitors the leachate, and enforces the liability if the company becomes insolvent?"
  • 03On timing: "Why is entering the market early better than entering the market well? Mongolia's strategic relevance will still be there in five years. What closes in the next year that doesn't close in year six?"
  • 04On community consent: "If communities directly affected by extraction object, does your model stop? If yes, what happens to your extraction program? If no, how is that meaningfully constrained?"
  • 05On diversification: "Why is adding another extractive sector real economic diversification rather than just a different label on the same model of commodity export without downstream leverage?"
🛡 Defensive — say these when Opp attacks you
  • 01On "why leave value underground": Value in the ground and benefit to Mongolia are two different claims. The mineral's existence is not the argument — the terms on which it is extracted and who captures the margin are. Leaving the option open until Mongolia has better leverage is not waste. It is strategy.
  • 02On "isn't your case really not now": The motion asks what Mongolia should do as a present policy choice. Right now, the threshold conditions for safe and beneficial extraction do not exist. Present non-authorization is a complete answer to a present-policy question. The present tense of the motion does not require defending a permanent prohibition — it requires defending the current policy choice. We do.
  • 03On "how does Mongolia get ready without starting": Not every capacity gap should be closed by exposing citizens to the risk in question. Regulatory capacity is built through international partnerships, legislative development, governance of adjacent lower-risk industries, and technical training. It does not require opening a high-harm sector as the learning mechanism when the cost of failure is irreversible.
  • 04On "would you oppose even a tightly regulated pilot": Yes, if the specific conditions that make a pilot safe — waste management protocols, independent monitoring, enforceable closure bonds, genuine liability mechanisms — are not currently in place. A pilot is not made safe by calling it a pilot. It is made safe by the safeguards around it. If those safeguards exist, the readiness condition has been met, and that changes the debate.
  • 05On "isn't this anti-development": No. This is anti-bad sequencing. Development that produces thin capture, irreversible environmental harm, and structural lock-in into weak contract terms is not development — it is a bad deal with a development label on it. Sequencing the entry correctly is the pro-development position.
🔴
Opp's Best POIs
▼
⚔️ Offensive — ask these to attack their case
  • 01On the absolutism trap: "Is your case 'not now' or 'never'? Because if it is 'not now,' you have conceded that conditions exist under which extraction is justified — and in that case, are you actually defending this absolute motion?"
  • 02On motion scope: "If the motion is really about conditions rather than a categorical prohibition, why are you defending this absolute motion rather than a conditional one? What word in the motion creates room for the threshold argument you are making?"
  • 03On pilot opposition: "Would you oppose a tightly regulated pilot with independent monitoring, a closure bond held by a neutral institution, strict liability provisions, and no automatic expansion clause? And if so, what specific condition would have to be met before any extraction is acceptable?"
  • 04On capacity building: "How does Mongolia build the specific sector competence it currently lacks — regulatory expertise, monitoring capacity, liability enforcement experience — without entering the sector at some level? What is the mechanism for getting ready without ever starting?"
  • 05On opportunity cost: "What specifically does Mongolia get from your refusal world that outweighs the strategic relationships, diplomatic capital, and revenue it forgoes? What is better about staying out of the market than going in under a constrained model?"
🛡 Defensive — say these when Prop attacks you
  • 01On "where is the value capture": Upstream margin is real even if it is not the largest margin in the chain. More importantly, entry creates the precondition for eventually developing downstream capacity. No country developed rare earth processing without first establishing extraction. Partial capture now, with the strategic relationships and institutional knowledge it builds, is a better starting position than zero capture and no relationships.
  • 02On "your safeguards aren't credible": The specific mechanisms we are defending — closure bonds held by neutral financial institutions, independent third-party monitoring with genuine reporting obligations, contractual liability with international arbitration, no automatic expansion — are legally and contractually definable. They do not require domestic enforcement capacity equivalent to Switzerland's. They require legal drafting capacity and willingness to include binding arbitration. Those conditions are available to Mongolia.
  • 03On "enter well not early": Supply chain relationships and partner commitments being formed now in response to REE supply chain restructuring are not indefinitely open. Countries and companies building new REE supply chains are making partner decisions now. A Mongolia that is not a plausible near-term supplier during this window will not be the first call when those decisions are made.
  • 04On "no expansion means permanent small scale": No automatic expansion means expansion requires a new authorization decision with a new assessment. That is different from a permanent scale cap. If the initial model demonstrates that governance, environmental management, and benefit capture are all working as designed, the case for authorized expansion becomes much stronger — made from an evidential base rather than from speculation.
  • 05On "another mine isn't diversification": We are not claiming REE extraction diversifies Mongolia out of mining. We are claiming it improves Mongolia's position within a mining-based economy by adding a more strategically relevant commodity. The choice available now is not between mining and a diversified post-extractive economy. It is between a mining profile dominated by declining-demand commodities and one that includes rising-demand ones. Within that realistic comparison, improvement counts.

🥊 Best Rebuttals

The strongest rebuttals directly name what the other side just argued, explain specifically why it fails, and connect the failure back to your overall thesis. The starred lines are your top three per side — have them ready from memory.

🔵
Prop Rebuttals
▼
  • ★"They proved the world wants rare earths. They did not prove Mongolia should bear this burden now." The logical gap that runs through Opposition's entire case is the assumption that global demand for REEs translates directly into benefit for Mongolian extractors. It does not. Global demand creates an opportunity. Whether Mongolia captures that opportunity in a way that benefits Mongolians depends entirely on the terms of extraction — who captures the margin, who bears the cost, who owns the liability. Opposition has argued extensively that rare earths are strategically important. They have spent much less time explaining why Mongolia specifically will receive good terms. Until they close that gap, the global importance argument does not answer the Mongolian benefit question.
  • ★"'Phased extraction' is softer language, not a safer mechanism." Opposition has described their model as phased, regulated, and constrained. These words describe an intention, not an outcome. The question is not whether Opposition intends their model to be safe — of course they do. The question is what specific structural features of their model prevent the governance failures that make REE extraction harmful. Calling something phased does not close tailings ponds. Calling something regulated does not create the monitoring infrastructure to detect leaching. Calling something constrained does not enforce the liability of an insolvent company after site abandonment. Opposition has provided description where they need to provide mechanism.
  • ★"Changing the mineral does not change the model if value capture stays thin." The comparative mining upgrade argument rests on the assumption that rare earths are meaningfully different from Mongolia's current commodities in terms of what Mongolia receives for producing them. But the low-capture problem is not specific to coal or copper — it is specific to raw material export without downstream leverage. If Mongolia extracts REE ore and ships it for processing elsewhere, the structural dependency is the same as with copper. The commodity has changed. The model has not. An upgrade within an unchanged model is not the improvement Opposition is claiming.
  • 4"Opposition's capacity-through-practice argument invites Mongolia to use its own communities as experimental subjects in a regulatory learning exercise. This is not an acceptable standard. Regulatory capacity is built through training, international partnership, legislative development, and governance of lower-risk industries — not by opening high-risk sectors and seeing what happens. Citizens should not bear the cost of closing institutional capacity gaps that could be closed another way."
  • 5"A state should not authorize before threshold conditions exist. This is not an extreme or unusual standard. It is how drug approval works. It is how nuclear facility licensing works. It is how environmental impact assessment works. You demonstrate you can govern the specific harms before you are authorized to create them. Opposition has not shown that standard has been met for REE extraction in Mongolia. That failure is sufficient to sustain the motion."
🔴
Opp Rebuttals
▼
  • ★"Prop attacks bad extraction. We defend controlled extraction." Almost everything Proposition has argued applies to unregulated, poorly governed, fully commercial extraction at scale. None of it directly addresses the constrained model Opposition has specified. The Lynas case in Malaysia, the DRC cobalt situation, the Zambian copper privatization — these are all examples of extraction without adequate safeguards. Opposition agrees those are bad. Opposition's model has safeguards. Proposition's rebuttals need to show why the safeguards specifically fail — not simply assert that extraction is risky in general.
  • ★"Readiness is not binary; controlled entry is enough." Proposition has described governance capacity as something Mongolia either has or does not have, and argued that until it has it, no extraction should proceed. But governance capacity exists on a spectrum, and different extraction models require different levels of it. A limited pilot with international monitoring and closure bonding requires less domestic institutional capacity than full commercial development at scale. The question is whether Mongolia's current institutional capacity is adequate for the constrained model — not whether it is adequate for the worst-case model. Proposition has not shown that the gap between what is required and what exists makes even the constrained model unsafe.
  • ★"Partial value capture still beats zero, and entry improves future bargaining." Proposition's optionality argument — that Mongolia can always enter later from a stronger position — assumes the strategic environment will remain equally open later as it is now. It will not. Supply chain partnerships are being formed now. Infrastructure investment decisions are being made now. A Mongolia that is absent during this period faces a more closed door, not an equally open one. The optionality argument has value, but it overestimates how long that option remains available at the same price.
  • 4"Proposition's actual case is caution, not prohibition. Read their case carefully and what you find is not an argument for permanent non-extraction — it is an argument that current conditions make extraction premature. The problem for Proposition is that they are defending a categorical motion. If their real argument is 'not under current conditions,' they have essentially conceded that conditions exist under which extraction is justified — and in that case, Opposition's one-credible-regulated-path argument succeeds, because Opposition has specified conditions that address Proposition's concerns. Proposition cannot simultaneously defend the absolute motion and advance a conditions-based case."
  • 5"Refusal is not neutral — it preserves zero leverage and zero participation. Proposition has presented non-authorization as a safe default, as if the risks of acting outweigh the risks of not acting. But non-authorization has its own risks and costs. Mongolia does not receive diplomatic capital from deposits it does not develop. It does not receive infrastructure investment from partners it cannot commit to. It does not build the regulatory knowledge that would make future entry easier. The cost of the refusal world is not zero — it is continued coal-and-copper dependence and continued absence from the partnerships forming around this market. Those are real costs, and Proposition has not accounted for them."

🚫 How to Lose This Debate

Most debates are lost not because the losing team made bad arguments, but because they made avoidable mistakes that undermined credibility or handed the other side their framing. These are the specific failure modes for this motion.

🔵 5 Ways Prop Loses
💀Sounding like "not now" without defending "should not." If Prop's case is clearly a threshold argument but they never directly engage with whether the absolute motion is defended by that threshold position, Opp runs the wording attack and wins it. Prop must address this explicitly and early: the present-policy standard is a complete answer to a present-policy motion. Say this in the opening speech, not as a rebuttal.
💀Becoming generically anti-mining. As soon as Prop makes broad arguments about the harms of extractive industry, the resource curse as a general pattern, or Mongolia's need for a post-mining economy, they have shifted from a specific mechanism-based case to a general ideological position. Judges distinguish between the two. Opp will exploit the shift by pointing out that the case applies to Mongolian mining broadly, which is not what the motion asks about.
💀Overclaiming Mongolia's incapacity. There is a difference between arguing that Mongolia's current governance is not specifically equipped for REE's particular demands, and arguing that Mongolia is incapable of governing anything. The former is defensible and specific. The latter is factually unsupported and politically off-putting. Stay at the level of the specific governance gap this specific industry creates.
💀Leaning too hard on speculative geopolitics. Arguments about Mongolia becoming a "pawn" in US-China competition, or about extraction making Mongolia a target for foreign pressure, sound compelling but are very difficult to cash out with specific mechanism in limited debate time. Use geopolitical arguments as illustration only, never as structural pillars.
💀Failing to answer the pilot project challenge. If Prop has not pre-answered the pilot question, Opp will deliver it as a POI and use the answer to define the round. The answer must be principled: a pilot is not safe by being called a pilot — it is safe when the specific safeguards around it are credible. Under current Mongolian conditions, those safeguards are not credibly in place.
🔴 5 Ways Opp Loses
💀Staying vague on what "phased extraction" actually means. If Opp describes their model using the words "phased," "regulated," "constrained," and "monitored" without specifying what any of those words mean in operational terms, Prop 2 will dismantle the model by pointing out that description is not mechanism. Every reference to the model must come with specific features: defined geographic scope, defined production limits, independent monitoring body type, closure bond structure, liability mechanism with international arbitration, no automatic expansion trigger.
💀Proving global importance but not Mongolian benefit. This is the most common failure mode on this motion. The argument that rare earths are strategically critical, globally in demand, and concentrated in China's supply chains is entirely consistent with Prop's case. None of that proves that Mongolia specifically will benefit from extraction under realistic current conditions. Opp must do the additional step of showing how Mongolia's specific position in the transaction produces positive net outcomes for Mongolians.
💀Overpromising jobs, technology transfer, or downstream industry. These promises are not credible in the short-to-medium term, and experienced judges will know it. More importantly, Prop will use any overclaiming to damage Opp's credibility across all its arguments. The discipline of not overpromising — making only claims that can be defended — is more important than the marginal persuasive effect of optimistic claims.
💀Letting Prop define the round as pure safety versus extraction. If the debate becomes "REE extraction is dangerous versus it is fine," Prop wins on ground, because the genuine risks are well-documented and Opp cannot plausibly claim otherwise. Opp's framing must be comparative throughout: not "extraction is safe" but "controlled extraction is better than refusal." That framing is much harder for Prop to attack because it doesn't require denying the risks.
💀Having no safeguards against indefinite or lazy extraction. If Opp is defending some form of extraction without specifying what governs its scope, duration, oversight, and endpoint, they are implicitly defending a position that could justify permanent large-scale extraction. State the safeguards explicitly in every speech: legal oversight, independent review, time limits, clinical justification — wait. In this debate: defined scope, independent monitoring, closure bonding, liability mechanisms, and no automatic expansion. Every single time the model is mentioned.
❌
5 Arguments That Sound Clever But Will Hurt You
Both sides — avoid these no matter how tempting they feel in the moment
▼
  • 1"Resource curse" as a label without mechanism (Prop). Saying Mongolia will suffer from the resource curse is not an argument — it is a label. It sounds sophisticated but is a placeholder for the real argument, which requires specifying the mechanism by which resource revenues undermine institutions in Mongolia's specific context. If you use this term, use it as the name for a mechanism you then explain, not as a self-standing claim.
  • 2"Green hypocrisy" as an Opp argument. The argument that Western countries calling for a green energy transition while opposing REE mining in developing countries are hypocritical has rhetorical appeal but does not help Opp win. It is a critique of Western policy, not a reason Mongolia should extract. It also invites Prop to respond: "Even if they are hypocrites, that does not mean Mongolia should make bad decisions." Avoid this entirely.
  • 3"Avoid becoming a pawn" as a Prop argument. The sovereignty argument — that extraction will make Mongolia a pawn in great-power competition — sounds compelling but is very difficult to cash out with specific mechanism. More importantly, it may work just as well against non-extraction: a country with known REE deposits that a great power wants may attract just as much pressure whether or not extraction is authorized. Avoid this as a structural argument.
  • 4Strong jobs claims for Opp. Claims that REE extraction will create significant Mongolian employment are almost always overstated for this type of industry. REE mining at the extraction stage is capital-intensive and creates fewer jobs relative to revenue than labor-intensive sectors. Using jobs as a major argument invites Prop to run the low-capture counter in its most damaging form and undermines Opp's credibility on the arguments that do hold.
  • 5Grand alternative-development theory for Prop in limited time. If Prop argues that Mongolia should instead invest in tourism, renewable energy, or a knowledge economy as an alternative to REE extraction, they are committing to defending a comprehensive development theory in a short speech. This almost never works. There is not enough time to establish the alternative with sufficient depth to make it credible, and Opp will spend thirty seconds pointing out all the reasons the alternative is not currently viable. Prop should not carry the burden of specifying a better path — their burden is to show this path is bad.
