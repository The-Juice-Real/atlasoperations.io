Motion Mongolia should allow foreign banks to operate in the country Full Brief

What Is This Debate Really About?

This debate is not about globalization as a philosophy. Almost everyone will accept that foreign banks can bring some benefits. The real fight is whether Mongolia should use regulated foreign-bank entry to fix a concentrated, underperforming banking system — or whether the state should hold the door shut because banking is strategic infrastructure and crisis control matters more than peacetime gains.

🔵 PROP MUST PROVE
✦That licensed, regulated, phased foreign-bank entry makes Mongolia's banking system more contestable, more capable, and harder for incumbents to coast in — overall better than the status quo.
✦That managed contestability produces real system improvement — not universal day-one gains for every borrower, but a meaningful upgrade to the overall system compared to concentrated incumbency with no competitive pressure.
✦That sovereignty means choosing the conditions of entry — not avoiding entry altogether. Mongolia's existing Banking Law already contemplates foreign-bank structures.
🔴 OPP MUST PROVE
✦That even under regulation, the first-order gains are too narrow — concentrated at the top end of the market — and the systemic and sequencing risks are serious enough that Mongolia should not allow entry now.
✦That crisis governability matters more than peacetime competition: practical control in stress is not guaranteed by formal legal authority when the parent institution is in another jurisdiction.
✦That the correct alternative is fix-then-open, with a specific and nameable readiness standard — not permanent exclusion — and that domestic reform tools have not yet been exhausted.
⚡ The Hidden Definitional Fight

When the motion says "allow foreign banks to operate," it does not specify terms. Proposition's strongest model: licensed, prudentially regulated, phased entry under Bank of Mongolia supervision. This is not deregulation. This is a controlled, rule-governed entry process using a legal framework Mongolia already has. Opposition's temptation: to treat the motion as if it means uncontrolled, open-door foreign bank presence — the kind of chaotic entry that caused problems in the 1990s.

If Opposition successfully implants the chaotic-entry image in the judge's mind, they win arguments they have not actually earned. Proposition must define the model in the first thirty seconds. Force Opposition to argue against controlled entry, not against a phantom of financial chaos. If Opposition cannot name what is specifically wrong with licensed, supervised entry and instead argues in abstract terms about sovereignty and crisis risk, they are debating their own imagination.

Defend by conceding that gains may start at the upper end, then insisting the burden is overall system improvement — not equal first-round benefit for every borrower. 🔵 Prop's Three Arguments

Each argument targets a different mechanism by which regulated foreign bank entry improves Mongolia's banking system. Together they show the system is better competitive, better capable, and more likely to actually reform — compared to the alternative of continued protected concentration. Every argument should connect back to that controlling frame.

01Contestability Against Concentration

Mongolia's banking sector is not a functioning competitive market. The top four banks control approximately 89 percent of total banking assets, which means customers — whether individuals, small businesses, or larger firms — have virtually no credible alternatives when a bank offers poor loan terms, slow service, or inadequate products. Incumbents in concentrated markets face almost no penalty for underperformance, because customers have nowhere else to go. They do not need to compete for business. They simply need to exist.

Foreign bank entry, even at modest initial scale, changes this calculus. When a licensed foreign bank begins operating in Mongolia, it offers the profitable customers and firms that domestic banks care most about a credible alternative. The threat of losing those clients — not the actual departure, but the credible possibility of it — is what forces incumbents to respond. They must improve pricing, service quality, loan turnaround times, and product offerings to retain business they previously held by default. This is the mechanism of contestability: the market becomes better not primarily because foreign banks serve everyone, but because their presence forces domestic banks to behave as if they had to compete.

📌 Real-World Example
Poland's banking sector opening during EU accession in the late 1990s and early 2000s produced measurable improvements in bank efficiency, product breadth, and technology adoption among domestic players. Poland's regulatory environment for the opening was supervised and phased — not a chaotic liberalization. The Polish banking sector today is among the more developed in Central and Eastern Europe, and much of that trajectory is connected to the competitive dynamics that foreign entry introduced. The key parallel for Mongolia: Poland's opening happened within a clear regulatory framework, not as deregulation.
💬 Say this: "Managed contestability beats protected concentration. The choice is not between a perfectly functioning domestic system and a risky foreign one — it is between a concentrated system with no competitive pressure and a regulated system where incumbents must actually compete."
⛓ So What? — Why This Wins the Motion
→Foreign bank entry gives profitable customers credible alternatives for the first time, creating competitive pressure on the incumbent banks that currently dominate 89% of the market
→Which means incumbents must improve pricing, service quality, and products to retain their most commercially important relationships
→Which means the overall quality and responsiveness of Mongolia's banking sector rises — not because foreign banks serve everyone, but because the market structure changes
→Which means the motion is met — because allowing foreign banks to operate is precisely the mechanism that creates this improvement. Without entry, the mechanism does not exist. The motion asks whether Mongolia should allow it; the answer is yes because system improvement follows.
⚖️ Why This Outweighs Opp's "Competition at the Top Doesn't Help Ordinary Borrowers"
Opposition will argue that competition in the premium segment does not automatically translate to benefits for SMEs, rural borrowers, or lower-income households. That observation is correct in the near term — and Prop should concede it honestly. The weight argument is that the burden for the motion is overall system improvement, not equal first-round distribution to every borrower. A banking sector where incumbents face genuine competition on their most profitable relationships — where they have a commercial incentive to improve — is structurally better than a banking sector where they face none. That structural improvement is real even if it begins at the upper end.
🧑‍⚖️ Silent Judge Objection
"Mongolia is not Poland. Doesn't institutional maturity, supervisory capacity, and market size matter for whether this mechanism actually works?"
Yes, they do — and none of those differences eliminates the mechanism. They affect the pace and scale of the competitive effect, but concentrated markets with no foreign entry remain concentrated markets with no competitive pressure regardless of country size. The implementation in Mongolia needs to be carefully phased, which is why Prop's model is regulated, supervised, phased entry — not instant full liberalization. The Poland example shows the direction; Mongolia's implementation would be appropriately calibrated to its institutional context.
🔴 Opp will say
Foreign banks in Sub-Saharan Africa concentrated in urban, corporate, and export-oriented segments — delivering benefits to those already well-served while withdrawing from marginal segments. This is the pattern Mongolia should expect.
🔵 Your defense
That evidence identifies an unequal distribution of benefits, not an absence of benefits. The motion does not require equal distribution — it requires overall system improvement. A banking sector where premium segments are more competitive still produces real economic value. And the African examples often involved much weaker regulatory environments than Mongolia's existing Banking Law provides.
02Product Depth and Capability Upgrade

Competition is not the only benefit of foreign bank entry. The second major benefit is capability. Foreign banks operating across multiple countries bring specialized expertise that Mongolia's banking sector currently handles poorly or does not handle at all: trade finance for import and export transactions, structured credit, sophisticated risk assessment, treasury management for larger firms, and project finance for capital-intensive investments. These are not abstract luxuries. For a commodity-exporting country like Mongolia — whose economy depends on coal, copper, and cashmere sold in international markets — access to competent trade finance and project lending is a real economic constraint, not a marginal inconvenience.

When foreign banks bring these capabilities into Mongolia's market, two things happen. First, Mongolian firms that previously could not access these services locally — and therefore either went without or paid to access them through overseas banking relationships — can now access them domestically. Second, domestic banks that observe foreign competitors successfully offering these services face pressure to develop similar capabilities over time. The capability upgrade therefore spreads beyond the foreign banks themselves, raising the ceiling of what the overall ecosystem can do.

📌 Real-World Example
Singapore's deliberate opening to major international banks from the 1970s onward — Citibank, HSBC, Deutsche Bank, and others — accelerated the development of sophisticated financial products and international expertise in its market. Singapore became the financial hub of Southeast Asia not despite foreign bank presence but in significant part because of it. Mongolia's ambitions are very different from Singapore's, but the underlying mechanism — foreign expertise raising the capability ceiling of a banking system — operates at any scale. Even a single well-capitalized foreign bank with strong trade finance operations changes what is available in the market.
💬 Say this: "We are not selling magic. We are selling a more capable banking system. Mongolia's commodity export economy needs trade finance and project lending that the current concentrated domestic sector cannot adequately provide."
⛓ So What? — Why This Wins the Motion
→Foreign banks bring trade finance, structured credit, and sophisticated underwriting capabilities that are currently unavailable or poorly developed in Mongolia's banking sector
→Which means Mongolian firms — particularly those engaged in commodity export and infrastructure investment — can access financial products that better match their actual needs
→Which means Mongolia's banking system can better support the country's real economic activity, deepening what the ecosystem as a whole can do
→Which means the motion is met — allowing foreign banks to operate is the mechanism through which these capabilities enter the system. Excluding foreign banks forecloses the capability upgrade entirely, and the economy bears the cost of that gap indefinitely.
⚖️ Why This Outweighs Opp's "Elite-First Gains" Response
Opposition's strongest counter is that capability improvements benefit only the already-bankable top end — large firms, trade-oriented businesses, financially sophisticated clients. Prop concedes the sequencing: yes, gains likely start at the upper end. The weight argument is that a country unable to properly finance its export sector, attract project finance for infrastructure, or offer competitive treasury services to its larger firms is economically constrained in ways that affect the whole economy. Better export financing means more export revenue. Better project finance means more infrastructure investment. Those effects do not stay contained at the top of the market.
🧑‍⚖️ Silent Judge Objection
"Mongolia's real banking problem is serving small businesses and rural borrowers — problems foreign banks won't touch. Isn't the capability argument solving the wrong problem?"
Mongolia's banking sector has multiple failures, and foreign banks directly address some of them while creating conditions — competitive pressure, capability raising — that help with others indirectly. SME finance and rural inclusion are real and serious problems, documented by the World Bank's 2024 report. Prop does not claim foreign banks will fix those directly. The argument is that the banking system has more than one failure, and addressing some of them is better than addressing none. The SME and rural finance problems require targeted domestic policy reform — but that is no reason to also leave the trade finance and competition problems unaddressed.
03Regulated Entry Beats Indefinite Protected Waiting

Opposition's implicit alternative is that Mongolia should first fix its domestic banking sector — improve supervision, strengthen governance, resolve structural distortions — and only then consider allowing foreign bank entry. The problem with this position is not that it is wrong about what needs fixing. Mongolia's supervisory capacity, corporate governance standards, and crisis management tools do need strengthening. The problem is that sheltered markets are precisely the environments in which reform is most easily postponed.

Incumbent banks in a protected market have strong incentives to resist reform that would expose them to real accountability — stricter governance requirements, stronger supervisory oversight, cleaner ownership structures. They can capture the political process, influence regulatory decisions, and delay change indefinitely. History is full of "fix it first" positions that never produced a fixed system because the protection of the market removed the pressure that would have forced the fixing. Foreign bank entry creates external pressure that is harder to neutralize through political influence. An incumbent that knows a foreign competitor will take its most profitable clients if it does not improve has a real commercial incentive to improve — not a regulatory incentive that can be lobbied away.

📌 Real-World Example
Mexico's banking sector before the 1994 peso crisis was domestically concentrated and poorly supervised — a protected system that had been reprivatized but lacked effective regulatory constraint. The crisis exposed severe weaknesses that had accumulated behind incumbent protection. The subsequent opening to foreign bank participation — including BBVA, Citigroup, and HSBC — introduced both capital and competitive pressure that accelerated the development of Mexico's regulatory framework. While the transition was difficult, the post-opening period produced a more stable and better-supervised banking sector than the pre-crisis protected period had managed to build. Mongolia's pre-entry situation has structural similarities: concentrated incumbency, developing supervision, and reform that has been easier to delay than to implement.
💬 Say this: "Sovereignty is choosing the rules of entry — not avoiding entry. Opposition's 'not now' has never become a 'here is when.' Protected markets do not reform themselves. The pressure has to come from somewhere."
⛓ So What? — Why This Wins the Motion
→Sheltered markets generate a political economy in which domestic banking reform is easiest to postpone — incumbents have every incentive to resist the governance and supervisory changes that would constrain them
→Which means "fix it first" positions, without a specific endpoint and enforcement mechanism, functionally become indefinite delay
→Which means regulated foreign bank entry — which creates commercial competitive pressure that incumbents cannot lobby away — is a more reliable path to a better banking sector than waiting for protected domestic reform
→Which means the motion is met — not merely because entry brings direct benefits, but because the alternative of continued protection is not a neutral holding position. It is an environment that actively resists the reform the sector needs.
⚖️ Why This Outweighs Opp's "Sequencing" Response
Opposition's strongest counter is that adding cross-border supervisory complexity before Mongolia's regulatory foundations are adequate is poor sequencing. This argument requires Opposition to name a specific, verifiable readiness standard and explain credibly why Mongolia will reach it without the pressure that foreign entry creates. If Opposition cannot name that threshold — and in a debate setting they typically cannot with specificity — then "fix it first" is not a policy position. It is an indefinite veto. The judge should put this question directly to Opp: ready when? According to whom? Enforced by what mechanism?
🧑‍⚖️ Silent Judge Objection
"But doesn't Mexico's example also show that opening before supervision is adequate can cause serious crises? Isn't that exactly what Opposition is warning about?"
The Mexico 1994 crisis happened largely in the domestic banking sector — poorly supervised domestic banks with weak governance, not foreign bank entry. The post-crisis opening to foreign participation was part of the recovery, not the cause of the problem. The lesson from Mexico is: concentrated, poorly supervised domestic banking is itself a crisis risk. The question is whether foreign bank entry under regulation reduces or increases that risk compared to continued protected concentration. The evidence from Mexico points toward foreign entry under regulation being part of the solution, not the problem.

🔴 Opp's Three Arguments

Opp's formula is consistent across all three arguments: acknowledge the real costs of concentration early, then show why the specific mechanism of foreign bank entry creates risks and distributional problems that outweigh the gains at this stage. Conceding that concentration is a problem is not a weakness — it makes every subsequent argument more credible.

01Banking Is Strategic Infrastructure — Crisis Control Matters More Than Peacetime Gains

Banking is not an ordinary service sector. It is strategic infrastructure in the same way that water, energy, and communications networks are strategic infrastructure. When banking fails, it does not affect only the direct participants in banking transactions — it cascades through every part of the economy that depends on credit, payment systems, and financial intermediation. That means almost everyone. Because of this, the most important test for any banking policy is not how it performs when things are going well. The most important test is whether the government can control the situation when things go badly.

Foreign banks operating in Mongolia introduce a specific and serious complication to crisis governance. When a foreign bank branch or subsidiary faces stress, the decisions that matter most — whether to inject liquidity, whether to reduce credit exposure, whether to retrench operations — are made not in Ulaanbaatar but in the parent institution's headquarters in another country. That parent institution's first obligation is to its own shareholders and its home-country regulator, not to the Bank of Mongolia. Legal authority on paper does not change operational priorities in practice. Mongolia can write whatever rules it likes about what a foreign bank must do in a crisis — but enforcing those rules against an institution whose parent is in another jurisdiction, in the time frame that banking crises demand, is genuinely difficult.

📌 Real-World Example
During the 2008 global financial crisis, foreign bank subsidiaries and branches operating in Eastern European countries — particularly the Baltic states and Hungary — began withdrawing credit lines and reducing local lending as their parent institutions tried to shore up capital at home. These credit contractions were driven not by local economic conditions but by decisions made in London, New York, and Frankfurt. The home governments of those parent banks had to negotiate emergency coordination frameworks — the Vienna Initiative — to prevent total credit collapse in Eastern European host countries. The host countries that had allowed foreign bank entry had no independent control over those decisions. Mongolia, smaller and without equivalent bilateral regulatory relationships, would face even greater difficulty in such a scenario.
💬 Say this: "The real test is not peacetime elegance. It is crisis governability. And in a crisis, who controls liquidity, retrenchment, and strategic priorities? Not the Bank of Mongolia. The parent institution."
⛓ So What? — Why This Wins the Motion
→Foreign bank operations create cross-border dependencies in the most critical decisions — liquidity provision, credit maintenance, operational continuity — at exactly the moment when those decisions matter most
→Which means Mongolia's government faces reduced practical control over the banking system during stress, precisely when control is most needed for macroeconomic stability
→Which means the motion introduces a systemic governance cost that formal regulation cannot fully neutralize — because legal authority and operational control in a crisis are different things
→Which means the motion fails the public-interest test — because allowing foreign banks to operate now, before Mongolia's crisis management capacity is adequately developed, imposes a systemic governance cost that outweighs the peacetime competitive gains Proposition has described.
⚖️ Why This Outweighs Prop's "Regulation Manages the Risk" Response
Proposition will argue that regulation keeps the risk manageable — that the Bank of Mongolia has legal authority to set conditions on foreign bank entry and enforce them. The weight argument is that legal authority and practical operational control are genuinely different things in a banking crisis. Mongolia can legally require a foreign bank to maintain liquidity ratios — but if the parent institution is itself in crisis and directing its Mongolia branch to repatriate capital, enforcing that requirement requires cross-border legal action in a time frame that banking crises do not allow. The Eastern Europe/Vienna Initiative example is not theoretical. It happened to host countries with more developed bilateral regulatory relationships than Mongolia currently has with any foreign bank's likely home country.
🧑‍⚖️ Silent Judge Objection
"Doesn't the crisis argument prove too much? By this logic, no country should ever allow foreign banks — they all face the same cross-border coordination challenge."
The challenge is much more acute for a small, commodity-dependent economy like Mongolia than for countries with larger, more diversified economies and more developed bilateral regulatory relationships. The asymmetry is real: the gains from foreign bank entry in Mongolia's market are smaller in absolute terms — the market is small — while the systemic governance costs are proportionally large for an economy where the banking sector accounts for over 90% of all financial sector assets. Countries with large, diversified economies and deep regulatory partnerships can manage the cross-border coordination challenge better. Mongolia is not yet in that position.
🔵 Prop will say
The Vienna Initiative example is exceptional — foreign bank entry doesn't normally produce crises. Most countries that have allowed foreign banks have not experienced Vienna-style emergency coordination situations.
🔴 Your defense
Correct — foreign bank entry does not cause crises. But when crises happen — for any reason, including commodity price shocks that Mongolia is especially vulnerable to — the presence of foreign banks whose crisis behavior is governed by external parent priorities makes the crisis harder to manage. Mongolia needs to ask not whether crisis is likely, but whether it has the tools to manage it when it comes.
02The Gains Are Concentrated at the Top — The Risks Are Borne by Everyone

Proposition's case rests on benefits that are real but narrow. Foreign banks entering Mongolia will most rationally target the most profitable and least risky segment of the market: large, creditworthy corporations; firms with international trade exposure; businesses with strong collateral and audited financial statements. These are the clients that generate the most revenue for the least risk. This is not a criticism of foreign banks — it is rational commercial behavior. But it means that the clearest, most immediate gains from foreign bank entry flow to a segment of the Mongolian economy that is already comparatively well served.

Meanwhile, the risks of foreign bank entry are distributed much more broadly. Systemic risk in banking does not respect the boundary between clients who benefit and clients who do not. If a foreign bank's operations contribute to a credit boom and subsequent contraction, or if a crisis at a parent institution triggers a withdrawal of credit lines, the economic consequences — reduced credit availability, tighter lending conditions, economic contraction — are felt across the whole economy, including by SMEs, rural borrowers, and lower-income households who received no direct benefit from the foreign bank's presence.

📌 Real-World Example
Research by the IMF and World Bank on foreign bank entry in Sub-Saharan African economies following liberalization in the 1990s and 2000s found consistently that foreign banks concentrated activity in urban areas, export-oriented sectors, and larger formally registered firms. They brought real benefits to those segments — better trade finance, improved products, lower pricing for premium clients. But they also accelerated the withdrawal of credit from marginal segments of the market, because their entry raised the opportunity cost for domestic banks to serve high-cost, lower-return clients. The gains were concentrated; the adjustment costs were dispersed.
💬 Say this: "Better premium banking is not the same as better public-interest banking. Proposition has shown that the top of the market improves. They have not shown that the system improves in the ways that matter most for the majority of Mongolians."
⛓ So What? — Why This Wins the Motion
→Foreign banks in Mongolia would most rationally produce their clearest early gains among large, creditworthy, trade-linked firms — the top segment of the market, already comparatively well served
→Which means the population of direct beneficiaries is narrow while systemic risk is distributed across the whole economy, including the most vulnerable segments
→Which means the motion asks Mongolia to socialize risk in exchange for gains that accrue primarily to those who least need the help
→Which means the public-interest threshold is not met — a policy that concentrates gains at the top and distributes risk broadly does not constitute the kind of system improvement that serves the country's overall interest, even if aggregate outcomes technically improve.
⚖️ Why This Outweighs Prop's "Overall System Improvement" Response
Proposition will argue that the burden is overall system improvement, not equal distribution, and that upper-end improvements still constitute system improvement. The weight argument is that burden arguments do not eliminate the relevance of distributional analysis. A judge applying genuine public-interest analysis must weigh not just whether aggregate outcomes improve, but whether the distribution of those outcomes is fair enough to justify the policy. When benefits are concentrated in an already-privileged segment while risks are socialized across everyone — including the most vulnerable — that distributional failure is itself a reason to reject the motion, not a secondary consideration.
🧑‍⚖️ Silent Judge Objection
"But the current system also fails SMEs and rural borrowers. Doesn't the status quo already impose exactly the distributional failure you are complaining about?"
Yes — and Opp must acknowledge this directly rather than pretending the status quo is fine. The current system does fail SMEs and rural borrowers. Mongolia's banking sector's treatment of SMEs is documented as constrained, collateral-heavy, and insufficiently patient. Opp's argument is not that the current system is good. It is that foreign bank entry is not the mechanism that will fix these specific problems — and that the mechanism most likely to address SME and rural finance is targeted domestic policy reform, not a competitive opening that will rationally direct foreign bank attention elsewhere. The status quo has costs. The question is whether those costs justify this specific reform mechanism at this specific time.
03Fix the Referee Before Inviting Stronger Teams

Opposition is not arguing that Mongolia should never allow foreign banks. The argument is about sequence. Mongolia's banking sector has known, documented weaknesses in supervision, governance, and structural integrity. The Bank of Mongolia's supervisory capacity is still developing. Corporate governance standards in the Mongolian banking sector have been identified as a concern by international assessors. Ownership structures in some domestic banks have involved conflicts of interest that proper governance frameworks should resolve.

Adding foreign bank operations to this environment — before those foundational weaknesses are adequately addressed — means asking a developing supervisory apparatus to simultaneously manage a harder oversight task. Cross-border bank supervision is more complex than domestic bank supervision. It requires coordination with foreign regulators, understanding of foreign institutional structures, and the ability to assess risks that originate outside Mongolia's legal and economic environment. If Mongolia's supervisors cannot yet reliably manage domestic banks well, adding foreign banks does not give them better tools — it gives them harder problems on a weaker foundation.

📌 Real-World Example
The Baltic states — Estonia, Latvia, and Lithuania — provide a constructive counter-model. They opened their banking sectors to foreign participation, predominantly Scandinavian banks, but did so after investing substantially in supervisory capacity and governance frameworks during the EU accession process. The result was a banking sector that, while heavily foreign-owned, was also well-supervised and more resilient than comparable post-Soviet banking sectors that opened without that foundation. The lesson is not "never open" — it is "the sequence of opening relative to supervisory readiness matters considerably for outcomes."
💬 Say this: "Fix the referee before inviting stronger teams. We support reform. We are asking for the correct sequence of reform — not permanent exclusion."
⛓ So What? — Why This Wins the Motion
→Mongolia's supervisory framework currently has identified weaknesses in capacity, governance oversight, and crisis management tools — weaknesses that international assessors have documented
→Which means adding cross-border bank supervision before those weaknesses are addressed creates a harder supervisory task in an already-strained environment
→Which means the risk of inadequate oversight is higher than it would be if entry happened after the supervisory foundation was strengthened — creating a sequencing problem even if the direction of reform is correct
→Which means the motion's timing is wrong — not the destination. Mongolia should allow foreign banks to operate, but not before the supervisory infrastructure is credibly adequate to manage them. Allowing them now, before that foundation is ready, is imprudent rather than merely cautious.
⚖️ Why This Outweighs Prop's "Protected Markets Don't Reform" Response
Proposition's strongest counter is that protected markets generate the political economy in which domestic reform is most easily postponed, so "fix it first" is functionally indefinite delay. Opp's response has two parts. First: there is a difference between competitive pressure and cross-border operational complexity. Domestic reform pressure can be created through stronger governance requirements, more demanding supervisory standards, and international technical assistance — without simultaneously adding the supervisory burden of overseeing foreign bank operations. Second: name the readiness standard. Opp should commit to a specific, verifiable endpoint — adequate supervisory capacity confirmed by IMF assessment, resolution framework in place, governance reforms implemented. These are specific, not indefinite. "Fix it first" becomes credible when "it" is named.
🧑‍⚖️ Silent Judge Objection
"Doesn't naming a readiness standard still amount to delay? And if domestic reform requires competitive pressure to happen, aren't you just describing a chicken-and-egg problem that Prop's entry mechanism solves?"
The delay is acknowledged and justified by asymmetric downside. If the delay costs Mongolia some competitive pressure for a few years while supervision is strengthened, the economy continues functioning as it does today — with the same costs as the current concentrated system. If premature opening leads to a crisis that the Bank of Mongolia cannot manage — or to cross-border complications during a commodity price shock — the consequences are macroeconomic destabilization, not mild inconvenience. Asymmetric downside justifies cautious sequencing. On the chicken-and-egg point: competitive pressure for domestic reform can come from sources other than foreign bank entry — governance requirements, ownership transparency mandates, IMF conditionality, and supervisory upgrade programs are all tools that create reform pressure without the cross-border supervisory complexity.

⚡ The 5 Big Clashes

These are the five points where the two sides most directly contradict each other. A judge deciding this debate will consciously or unconsciously resolve these clashes and award the ballot to the side that won more of them. Know which clashes your side has the easier burden on — and which ones you are likely to lose so you can minimize their importance.

Even FightClash 1 — Contestability vs. Crisis Governability
Prop Must Win This By Showing
That the competitive improvements produced by regulated foreign bank entry are real, meaningful, and durable enough to justify accepting the additional complexity of cross-border banking supervision. Prop does not need to prove that crisis risk disappears. They need to prove that the crisis risk under regulated entry is manageable and is outweighed by the structural benefits of competition. Specifically: that the Bank of Mongolia's existing legal framework is credible enough to manage entry safely, and that the alternative of continued concentrated incumbency is itself a crisis risk that Opp has not accounted for.
Opp Must Win This By Showing
That practical control over banking behavior during stress — not formal legal authority, but real operational control — is genuinely compromised by foreign bank operations. Opp needs the Eastern Europe/Vienna Initiative story to be concrete, not abstract. The argument must show specifically how a commodity-price shock or parent-institution crisis would play out differently in a Mongolia with foreign banks versus a Mongolia without them — and why that difference is large enough to outweigh the peacetime contestability gains.
⚖️ How a Judge Decides This Clash
The judge asks two questions in sequence. First: is the contestability gain real? If Prop cannot show a credible mechanism by which foreign bank entry changes incumbent behavior — not just theoretically but in a way that applies to Mongolia's market structure — the gain is speculative. Second: is the crisis risk qualitatively different from the risks that already exist in a concentrated banking system? Concentration itself is a crisis risk — Iceland's entirely domestic banking system collapsed spectacularly in 2008. If Opp treats the current concentrated system as risk-free while treating foreign entry as risky, a judge who knows banking history will penalize that asymmetry. Prop loses this clash if they claim regulation fully resolves the crisis coordination problem. Opp loses it if they treat current concentration as risk-free.
Opp Has Easier BurdenClash 2 — Elite-First Gains vs. Public-Interest Reform
Prop Must Win This By Showing
That the burden is overall system improvement — not equal distribution — and that even if gains start at the upper end, the overall banking system is meaningfully better with foreign bank entry. Prop should also argue that upper-end improvements have economy-wide effects through trade finance, investment, and competitive pressure on incumbents. A banking sector that better serves exporters means more export revenue. Better project finance means more infrastructure. Those effects travel down through the economy.
Opp Must Win This By Showing
That the concentration of gains at the top — combined with the broad distribution of systemic risk — represents a distributional failure serious enough to outweigh aggregate improvement claims. Opp must show this is not abstract but a predictable, documented pattern based on the Sub-Saharan Africa evidence. Make the judge feel the specific unfairness: the people who benefit first are the largest firms; the people who absorb the risk if something goes wrong are everyone.
⚖️ How a Judge Decides This Clash
This is the clash where the judge's prior commitments matter most. A judge who thinks about policy in terms of aggregate efficiency will find Prop's argument more compelling. A judge who thinks about policy in terms of fairness and who bears risk will find Opp's argument more compelling. Prop wins this clash by anchoring the judge's frame to overall system improvement as the relevant standard. Opp wins by getting the judge to apply a distributional lens. Prop loses if they try to argue foreign banks will directly reach SMEs and rural borrowers in the near term — they will not, and overclaiming destroys credibility. Opp loses if they fail to acknowledge that the current system is also failing SMEs and rural borrowers — if Opp implies the status quo serves the public interest, a well-informed judge will find that implausible and discount the argument.
Even FightClash 3 — Reform Pressure vs. Reform Sequencing
Prop Must Win This By Showing
That domestic reform of Mongolia's banking sector is genuinely more likely to happen with the external competitive pressure of foreign bank entry than without it. This requires a credible account of why incumbent banks would resist reform in a protected market but could not resist reform under competitive pressure — and why other sources of reform pressure (regulatory requirements, IMF conditionality) have proven insufficient without competitive pressure alongside them.
Opp Must Win This By Showing
That domestic reform can be achieved through better governance requirements, stronger supervision, and targeted regulatory intervention without adding cross-border operational complexity — AND that Opp has a specific, nameable readiness standard, not just a vague call for preparation. "Fix it first" without naming what "fixed" means is not a policy position. Opp must commit to a specific endpoint.
⚖️ How a Judge Decides This Clash
The judge weighs this primarily by asking which side has the more credible account of how reform actually happens in concentrated banking markets. This is an empirical question and the evidence is mixed — some sectors reformed without foreign entry, others accelerated reform because of it. The judge must decide which story applies more credibly to Mongolia's specific situation. Prop loses if they cannot explain specifically why Mongolia's domestic political economy would not produce reform without foreign bank entry — the general claim that "protected markets delay reform" needs to be connected to Mongolia's specific incumbent-regulator relationships. Opp loses if they cannot name a specific, credible reform path — "fix it first" without a defined endpoint is an indefinite veto, and the judge will penalize it as such.
Opp Has Easier BurdenClash 4 — Sovereignty as Rule-Setting vs. Sovereignty as Stress-Time Control
Prop Must Win This By Showing
That sovereignty — Mongolia's right to govern its own economy — is not compromised by regulated foreign bank entry. The argument is that choosing who enters, under what conditions, and subject to what rules is itself an exercise of sovereignty. Mongolia's Banking Law already contemplates foreign-bank structures. Licensed, supervised entry is Mongolia's sovereign decision, not an erosion of sovereign control. Sovereignty includes the right to admit entrants under rules that Mongolia sets.
Opp Must Win This By Showing
That formal legal sovereignty is not the same as practical operational control during a crisis. Being able to write rules about what foreign banks must do is different from being able to enforce those rules against an institution whose parent is operating under a different regulatory regime in a different country. The Vienna Initiative example is the concrete proof: host countries had formal legal authority and still could not prevent foreign banks from reducing credit in ways driven by parent institution priorities.
⚖️ How a Judge Decides This Clash
The judge asks: when practical sovereignty matters most — in a crisis — does Mongolia have the tools to exercise it? If formal regulatory authority is a credible approximation of operational control, Prop wins. If practical operational control requires more than formal authority — requires actual leverage over the institution's decision-making in real time — Opp wins. Prop loses this clash if they treat formal legal authority as equivalent to operational control in a crisis — any judge who understands how banking crises work will know these are different things. Opp loses this clash if they use "sovereignty" as a slogan without explaining the specific mechanism by which formal legal authority breaks down — vague sovereignty appeals are rhetoric, not argument.
Prop Has Easier BurdenClash 5 — Status Quo Cost vs. Reform Risk
Prop Must Win This By Showing
That the current concentrated banking system is not a neutral, stable baseline. It imposes real costs on the Mongolian economy: higher pricing on loans, lower service quality, product gaps, and structural fragility from concentration itself. Those costs are ongoing and significant. The status quo is not safe — it is a slow drain on economic performance and a structural vulnerability if incumbent banks fail. Concentration risk is crisis risk too.
Opp Must Win This By Showing
That the risks associated with premature foreign bank entry are more serious than the costs of the current concentrated system — not that the current system is fine, but that the specific risks of the proposed change outweigh the specific costs of the current situation. Opp cannot win this clash by treating the status quo as acceptable. They must show the change is specifically worse than the status quo, not just that the status quo has problems.
⚖️ How a Judge Decides This Clash
This is the clash where Prop has the easier burden, and a good Prop team should exploit this. The judge weighs it by asking: has Opp given me a credible reason to prefer the current situation, given that both sides acknowledge it has real problems? If Opp cannot do this — if their position is simply "the risks of change are high" without a compelling account of what makes the status quo acceptable — Prop wins by default. Prop loses this clash if they understate the risks of reform, making their case sound like an advertisement rather than a serious policy argument. Opp loses — as they very frequently do on this clash — if they allow the debate to proceed as though the status quo is cost-free. Concentration is costly. Indefinite delay is costly. Opp must engage with these costs seriously.

💬 Best Questions to Ask

A good POI exposes a gap in the other side's argument or forces them to say something they would rather not say out loud. Even if declined, the judge hears the question — so a well-chosen POI does work regardless of whether it is accepted. Have your top three ready from memory so you can deliver them calmly and confidently without looking down.

🔵
Prop's Best POIs
▼
⚔️ Offensive — ask these to attack their case
  • 01If this is not permanent delay — if you genuinely support foreign bank entry at some point — can you tell us specifically what readiness looks like and when Mongolia will get there?
  • 02When you argue for protecting Mongolia's banking sector from foreign competition, are you protecting the stability of the financial system — or are you protecting the profitability of the four banks that control 89% of assets?
  • 03If foreign bank entry is licensed, supervised, and phased under Bank of Mongolia control — using Mongolia's existing Banking Law — why are you arguing as if the motion means deregulation?
  • 04Why is it impossible for Mongolia to improve its supervisory capacity alongside a carefully phased entry process, rather than requiring one to come before the other?
  • 05Given that Mongolia's top four banks control 89% of assets, what competitive mechanism — without foreign bank entry — will produce the pricing improvements and service quality gains that your side has not disputed are needed?
🛡 Defensive — say these when Opp attacks you
  • 01When Opp says "foreign banks will cherry-pick premium clients": Some likely will, at least initially. That still changes the parts of the market that incumbent banks care most about. When a premium corporate client threatens to leave for a foreign competitor, Golomt Bank's revenue is at risk. That commercial threat forces incumbents to respond. Contestability does not require foreign banks to serve everyone — it requires that they create a credible threat to the most profitable relationships.
  • 02When Opp says "you can't prove SME gains": Correct — and we are not trying to. Our burden is overall system improvement, not guaranteed benefits for every market segment on day one. A more competitive and more capable banking system is a better banking system even if some improvements take time to diffuse through the market.
  • 03When Opp says "this surrenders sovereignty": Sovereignty is the right to choose the conditions of entry — not the obligation to exclude. Mongolia choosing to license a foreign bank under specific capital requirements, supervisory obligations, and behavioral rules is an exercise of sovereignty. The alternative — permanently excluding foreign banks because the government cannot trust its own regulatory framework — is an admission that the framework is not working.
  • 04When Opp uses the 2008 Eastern Europe example: The Vienna Initiative was needed because host countries had weaker bilateral regulatory relationships with the home countries of their foreign banks. The lesson is about the importance of strong regulatory coordination frameworks — which Mongolia can build as part of a phased entry process. It is not a lesson that foreign bank entry is impossible to manage safely.
  • 05When Opp says "why not just reform domestic banks?": Because sheltered markets generate precisely the political economy in which domestic banking reform is easiest to postpone. Incumbent banks with no competitive pressure have no commercial penalty for resisting stronger governance requirements. Domestic reform is easier to achieve when incumbents have a commercial reason to want to improve rather than only a regulatory reason to resist getting caught.
🔴
Opp's Best POIs
▼
⚔️ Offensive — ask these to attack their case
  • 01Under your model, which segment of the Mongolian economy benefits first — small and medium businesses and ordinary borrowers, or premium corporates and trade-linked firms?
  • 02If the gains from foreign bank entry flow primarily to already-bankable top-tier clients, why should the full Mongolian public absorb the systemic risk of cross-border banking complexity?
  • 03What specific problem are you solving with foreign bank entry that stronger domestic regulation — better governance requirements, improved supervisory standards, reformed lending rules — could not solve directly?
  • 04In a genuine financial crisis, who has the final practical priority over a foreign bank's decisions about liquidity and credit in Mongolia — the Bank of Mongolia, or the parent institution's board and home regulator?
  • 05Can you point to a comparable economy where foreign bank entry produced measurable improvement in financial inclusion for small borrowers — not just premium clients — within a reasonable time frame?
🛡 Defensive — say these when Prop attacks you
  • 01When Prop says "when exactly is Mongolia ready?": When Mongolia has completed an IMF Financial Sector Assessment Program evaluation confirming that supervisory capacity is adequate; when the Banking Law's governance provisions have been implemented and assessed; and when Mongolia has a credible bank resolution framework in place. These are specific. The timeline for achieving them is Mongolia's to determine — but the endpoint is nameable and not indefinite.
  • 02When Prop says "are you protecting incumbents?": No — and we demonstrate this by explicitly acknowledging that concentration is a real problem requiring real solutions. The distinction is between supporting reform — yes — and supporting this specific mechanism of reform at this specific moment — no. We pair our caution about foreign bank entry with a concrete domestic reform path. That is not incumbent protection; it is correct sequencing.
  • 03When Prop says "concentration requires real competition": We agree. But not all entry solves concentration well. Elite-focused foreign bank entry may deepen market segmentation rather than address the core concentration problem, because the segment where foreign banks compete may not significantly overlap with the segment where concentration causes most harm to ordinary Mongolians. Contestability in one segment is not contestability across the market.
  • 04When Prop says "regulation can manage foreign banks": On paper, in peacetime, partly — yes. The limitation is in practice, during stress, in the time frame that banking crises demand. We are not saying regulation is useless. We are saying formal legal authority and practical operational control are different things when a parent institution in another jurisdiction is making the real decisions.
  • 05When Prop says "are you anti-foreign investment?": No. This is selective caution in a strategic sector during a specific developmental window. Our argument applies specifically to bank operations — not to foreign direct investment generally, not to trade, not to foreign participation in other sectors of Mongolia's economy. Banking's strategic infrastructure status makes it a special case, not a proxy for broader economic openness.

🥊 Best Rebuttals

The best rebuttals name what the other side has just said, explain specifically why it fails, and connect the failure back to your side's controlling thesis. The starred lines are your top three per side — have them ready to deliver from memory.

🔵
Prop Rebuttals
▼
  • ★"Risk existing is not risk defeating the motion." Opposition has proved that cross-border banking involves risks that do not exist in a purely domestic system. That is true. But the motion does not require Prop to prove that regulated foreign bank entry is risk-free. It requires Prop to prove that the overall banking system is better with regulated entry than without it. Every banking policy involves risk. The relevant question is: which risks are more serious — the risks of regulated foreign bank entry, or the risks of continued concentrated incumbency with no competitive pressure? Opposition has not shown that the risks of entry are worse than the risks of the status quo. Until they do, risk existing is not risk defeating the motion.
  • ★"Cherry-picking concedes the contestability argument." When Opposition says foreign banks will cherry-pick premium clients, they are conceding that foreign banks will enter the most commercially important segment of the market — the segment that incumbent banks care about most and work hardest to retain. That is exactly where the contestability effect operates. The incumbents do not need to lose those clients — they need to face the credible possibility of losing them. A foreign bank competing for the largest and most profitable corporate clients in Mongolia creates a real commercial threat to the domestic banks whose profitability depends on those clients. Opposition cannot simultaneously argue that foreign banks will cherry-pick premium clients and argue that foreign bank entry will have no competitive effect on incumbents.
  • ★"Opposition has given no endpoint." Opposition has argued for sequencing — for Mongolia to fix its domestic banking sector before allowing foreign bank entry. But at no point in this debate has Opposition named a specific, verifiable standard for when Mongolia would be ready, who would assess readiness, or what happens if that assessment concludes readiness has not been reached. Without those specifics, "fix it first" is not a policy position — it is an indefinite veto dressed up as responsible sequencing. Ready when? According to whom? Enforced by what mechanism? If Opposition cannot answer those questions, their position amounts to permanent exclusion.
  • 4"Strategic infrastructure is an argument for better competition, not protected concentration. Opposition has argued that banking is strategic infrastructure and therefore must be protected from foreign entry. But strategic infrastructure that is poorly run, concentrated among a small number of players, and immune from competitive pressure is not less dangerous because it is domestic — it is more dangerous. If banking is truly strategic, that is a reason to ensure it is competitive, capable, and well-managed — not a reason to leave its current dominant players comfortable in their concentration."
  • 5"The comparative burden is system improvement, not equal day-one benefits. Opposition spent significant time arguing that foreign bank entry will not immediately improve things for SMEs and rural borrowers. We acknowledge this. But the motion does not require instant equal benefits. It asks whether Mongolia should allow foreign banks to operate — and the standard for yes is whether doing so makes the overall banking system better. A more competitive, more capable system that serves the upper market better and creates pressure on incumbents to improve is a better system than the current concentrated, underperforming alternative."
🔴
Opp Rebuttals
▼
  • ★"Better premium banking is not the same as public-interest banking reform." Proposition has argued that the overall banking system improves with foreign bank entry. But the specific improvements they have described — more competitive pricing for premium clients, better trade finance, more sophisticated underwriting — are improvements that primarily benefit clients who are already comparatively well served. When Proposition says the system is "better," they mean it is better for a specific segment. Mongolia's most serious banking failures — the constraint on SME finance, the collateral-heavy lending that locks out smaller borrowers, the limited reach into rural areas — are not addressed by foreign banks whose commercial incentives point them toward exactly the opposite segment. Proposition has proved that premium banking improves. They have not proved that Mongolia's banking system improves in the ways that matter most for the majority of Mongolians.
  • ★"Legal control on paper is not crisis control in practice." Proposition has argued that regulation preserves sovereignty — that because Mongolia can set the rules for foreign bank entry, it retains control. This conflates legal authority with operational control. Mongolia can legally require that a foreign bank maintain certain liquidity ratios and follow certain operational rules. But if that bank's parent institution, operating under a different regulatory regime in a different country, is directing its Mongolia branch to retrench capital during a crisis, enforcing Mongolia's legal requirements requires cross-border legal action in a time frame that banking crises do not allow. That is not a theoretical concern — it is what happened to Eastern European host countries during the 2008 crisis, before the Vienna Initiative created emergency coordination mechanisms that Mongolia has no equivalent of.
  • ★"More entrants do not automatically create broad competition." Proposition's contestability argument assumes that introducing foreign banks creates competition that benefits the broad market. But contestability works specifically when new entrants threaten incumbents for the clients that drive incumbent behavior. If foreign banks exclusively serve premium corporates and trade-linked firms, domestic incumbent banks may simply partition the market — ceding premium corporate banking to foreign competitors while retaining, unchallenged, their dominant position in the retail, SME, and mid-market segments where the majority of Mongolian banking customers are. The contestability effect is real in the premium segment. It is not automatically real across the market.
  • 4"Proposition's examples don't transfer. Poland's banking opening happened within the EU accession process — an unusually powerful external governance framework with enormous institutional reform incentives. Singapore's opening happened in an environment of exceptionally strong regulatory capacity. Neither transfers directly to Mongolia's context. The gains Prop is describing are the gains that accrue when entry is well-managed in a strong institutional environment. Mongolia is not yet that environment — which is precisely our sequencing argument."
  • 5"Domestic reform pressure can come from governance without cross-border risk. Proposition argues that domestic reform is impossible without the external competitive pressure of foreign bank entry — that protected markets never reform themselves. This overstates the case. Domestic reform pressure can be created by strengthening governance requirements, requiring independent boards, mandating transparent ownership structures, and strengthening supervisory standards in ways that impose real accountability on incumbent banks. Countries have successfully reformed concentrated banking sectors without foreign bank entry. Mongolia has not yet exhausted those tools."

Each speaker has one primary job. The biggest mistake teams make is when speakers drift into each other's territory — repeating what has already been said, or front-loading material that lands better later. Know your lane and stay in it.

🎯 Opening & Closing Lines

The opening line is the first thing the judge hears your captain say. The closing bullets are the last things the judge hears before they deliberate. Both should be memorized and delivered without notes.

🔵 Prop Captain — Opening Line
"This debate is about regulated entry, not reckless opening. The question before us is whether Mongolia should use its existing legal authority to license foreign banks under Mongolian supervision — not whether it should throw open its doors without rules. We say yes: managed contestability produces a better banking system than protected concentration. Mongolia's top four banks control 89% of assets and face no meaningful competitive pressure. That is not a neutral baseline. It is a structural failure. Regulated foreign bank entry is the mechanism that changes it."
🔴 Opp Captain — Opening Line
"The question is not whether foreign banks can be licensed. Of course they can. The question is whether allowing them to operate in Mongolia's banking sector now — before the supervisory foundations are ready, and when the clearest gains flow to the top of an already unequal market — is wise policy for the Mongolian public interest. We support banking reform. We support competition. We are asking for the right sequence of reform, not permanent exclusion. And we will show you that the sequence matters enormously for who actually benefits and who absorbs the risk."
🔵 Prop — Closing Bullets
✦This was about regulated entry, not reckless opening. Opp debated a phantom of financial chaos. Prop's model is licensed, supervised, phased — using Mongolia's existing Banking Law. Opp never named what is specifically wrong with that model.
✦Opp proved risk exists. They did not prove that exclusion is better than regulated entry. Every banking system involves risk. Compare this risk against the risk of concentrated incumbency — not against zero.
✦"Cherry-picking" concedes the contestability argument. If foreign banks take the most profitable corporate clients, incumbents face a real commercial threat. That threat changes behavior. That is exactly how contestability works.
✦Opp's "fix it first" never became a real readiness standard. They never told you when Mongolia would be ready, who would assess it, or what mechanism produces domestic reform in a protected market without competitive pressure. That silence is the vote.
✦Managed contestability beats protected concentration. The side that named a concrete mechanism for a better banking system, backed by verified evidence, with no comparably credible alternative named on the other side — wins this round.
🔴 Opp — Closing Bullets
✦Banking is strategic infrastructure. The real test is not peacetime elegance — it is crisis governability. In a crisis, who controls liquidity and credit decisions in Mongolia? Not the Bank of Mongolia. The parent institution in another country.
✦Prop's clearest gains flow to the largest, most creditworthy firms in Mongolia first. The people who absorb the risk if something goes wrong are everyone. That distributional asymmetry is not a side issue — it is the public-interest test for this motion.
✦Public-interest banking reform is not the same as premium competition improvement. Prop proved the top of the market gets better. They did not prove that Mongolia's banking system improves in the ways that matter most for most Mongolians.
✦Sequencing is not protectionism when the downside is systemic. We named the readiness standard: adequate supervisory capacity confirmed by IMF assessment, a credible resolution framework, governance reforms implemented. That is specific. That is not indefinite delay.
✦Do not trade public-interest sequencing for elite-first banking gains. Vote for the side that took the public-interest question seriously — not the side that reframed it as a competition policy debate.

🚫 How to Lose This Debate

Most debates are lost not because the losing team made bad arguments, but because they made avoidable mistakes that handed the other side their framing. These are the specific traps that teams consistently fall into on this motion.

🔵 5 Ways Prop Loses
💀Claiming foreign banks will directly fix SME or rural finance near-term. This is almost certainly false, immediately demolishable, and will make the rest of the case look equally overblown. Your scope is overall system improvement through contestability. Stay there.
💀Treating regulation as a complete answer to crisis risk. Claiming regulation fully resolves the cross-border coordination problem is technically false and any judge with banking knowledge will know it. Argue that the risk is manageable and outweighed — not that it disappears.
💀Sounding like the argument is "foreign is better." If Prop's speeches give any impression that foreign institutions are inherently superior to Mongolian ones, they have made the wrong argument. The argument is about concentrated versus contested markets — not national origin of institutions.
💀Letting Opposition define the ballot as sovereignty versus globalization. This is Opp's most favorable framing and it is false to the debate. The correct frame is whether regulated banking sector entry improves a specific market structure. Keep it specific and technical, not philosophical.
💀Running five weak arguments instead of three strong ones. Investor confidence, digital services, talent spillovers, financial inclusion, income growth — all of these are soft and all can be demolished. Three mechanically rigorous arguments defended under pressure beat five soft claims every time.
🔴 5 Ways Opp Loses
💀Sounding like you are defending incumbent banks. This is Opp's most dangerous failure mode. One moment of appearing to protect the market position of Mongolia's existing dominant banks destroys credibility for the entire round. State your pro-reform posture in the first sentence of the case and repeat it whenever Prop attacks.
💀Offering delay with no endpoint. "Fix it first" without a specific, verifiable readiness standard is not a policy position — it is an indefinite veto. Name the endpoint: adequate supervisory capacity per IMF FSAP assessment, resolution framework in place, governance reforms implemented. Be specific or Prop wins the sequencing clash by default.
💀Using "sovereignty" as a slogan without a mechanism. "Banking is sovereign infrastructure" requires a specific argument about what practical control breaks down and how. Without the mechanism — who controls what decision in a crisis — sovereignty appeals are rhetoric, not argument.
💀Ignoring the costs of concentration. A banking sector with 89% of assets in four banks is not a neutral baseline. If Opp debates as though the status quo is risk-free and cost-free, Prop simply points to the concentration facts and asks why those costs are not equally concerning. Acknowledge the costs. Then explain why this specific mechanism at this specific time is the wrong response.
💀Debating an extreme Prop model instead of the regulated one. If Opp spends their time arguing against unregulated, open-border foreign bank entry, they are not engaging with the model Prop has defined. A Prop team that clearly defines their model at the start will be able to point out repeatedly that Opp is arguing against a position nobody is defending. Engage with the controlled, phased, Bank of Mongolia-supervised model.

5 Arguments That Sound Clever But Will Hurt You
Both sides — avoid these no matter how tempting they feel in the moment
▼
  • 1"Foreign banks will save Mongolian SMEs." This is factually false as a near-term prediction. Foreign banks entering a new market have no commercial incentive to lend to small, information-poor borrowers with limited collateral. Making this claim gives Opp a devastating rebuttal and makes everything else Prop says seem equally overblown.
  • 2"Domestic banks are inherently safer because they are local." Factually false and easily demolished. Iceland 2008 — a banking collapse driven entirely by domestic banks — is the instant refutation. Local ownership does not produce better governance or better crisis outcomes. Opp should argue for the crisis governability mechanism, not for the unsustainable claim that domestic institutions are inherently more stable.
  • 3"Investor confidence and openness will attract more foreign direct investment." The causal chain from "foreign banks allowed" to "Mongolia attracts significantly more FDI" is long, contested, and impossible to verify in a debate context. It makes Prop sound like an advertisement for economic liberalization rather than a grounded argument about banking sector improvement. Leave this out entirely.
  • 4"Profit repatriation is the main problem with foreign banks." This proves too much — by the same logic, Mongolia should not allow any foreign business to operate in the country. As a standalone argument it makes Opp sound anti-foreign investment in a way that undermines the entire case. It is a supporting concern at most, not a primary argument.
  • 5"More bank logos equal more competition." The number of institutions is not the same as the competitiveness of the market for all segments. If foreign banks concentrate in the premium segment while domestic incumbents remain dominant elsewhere, the market has more logos without having more competition for most customers. Prop must make the specific contestability argument — that competition for the premium segment changes incumbent behavior throughout their operations — not the superficial "more banks equals more competition" claim.

Your 2 Core Arguments
